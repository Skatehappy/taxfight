Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Florida

Florida law provides multiple property tax exemptions for veterans, each with its own eligibility rules. Under Fla. Stat. § 196.081, a veteran with a 100% service-connected total and permanent disability is entitled to a full exemption from ad valorem taxes on their homestead. The exemption also extends to surviving spouses who have not remarried, provided the property was the homestead at the time of the veteran's death. Fla. Stat. § 196.082 grants a discount on homestead taxes for veterans age 65 or older who are partially and permanently disabled from service-connected causes, with the discount equal to the percentage of the veteran's disability rating from the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs. Fla. Stat. § 196.24 provides an additional $5,000 exemption for veterans with a service-connected disability of 10% or more, and Fla. Stat. § 196.091 covers exemptions for veterans confined to wheelchairs. To qualify, applicants must generally file Form DR-501 (and supporting forms such as DR-501DV) with the county property appraiser by March 1 of the tax year, along with VA documentation showing the disability rating, proof of Florida residency, and homestead status. County property appraisers can deny applications for missing documentation, late filing, failure to establish homestead, or disputes over the disability percentage. When that happens, the appraiser must issue a written notice of denial under Fla. Stat. § 196.193, and the veteran has the right to challenge that denial. Appeals proceed through the county Value Adjustment Board under Fla. Stat. § 194.011, with further review available in circuit court under Fla. Stat. § 194.171.

How a Demand Letter Works in Florida

A demand letter to the Florida county property appraiser is often the fastest and least expensive way to reverse an improper denial of a veteran exemption. Many denials stem from clerical mismatches between the VA rating letter and the appraiser's records, missing surviving spouse documentation, or a misunderstanding about whether the disability is service-connected and total. A clear letter that cites the controlling statute, attaches the VA disability award letter, DD-214, proof of homestead, and any prior approvals can prompt the appraiser to correct the record administratively without a VAB petition. Your demand letter should: (1) identify the parcel and tax year in dispute; (2) cite the specific exemption statute that applies (§ 196.081 for 100% disabled, § 196.082 for the age-65 combat-related discount, or § 196.24 for the $5,000 exemption); (3) attach documentary proof of eligibility; (4) request a written response within 14 days; and (5) preserve the 30-day VAB deadline by stating that you are not waiving any appeal rights. Sending the letter by certified mail creates a paper trail useful at the VAB hearing or in circuit court. Even if the appraiser refuses to reverse, the letter forces them to put their reasoning in writing, which often exposes weaknesses you can exploit at the hearing. Many counties resolve veteran exemption disputes informally once the documentation gap is cured.

Procedural Notes for Florida

Petitions to the Value Adjustment Board must be filed within 30 days of the TRIM notice (typically mailed in mid-August) or within 30 days of a denial of exemption under Fla. Stat. § 196.193. The VAB filing fee is capped at $15 per petition under Fla. Stat. § 194.013. If the VAB rules against you, you may file suit in circuit court within 60 days under Fla. Stat. § 194.171; this deadline is jurisdictional and cannot be extended. Florida small claims court (limit $8,000) is generally not the proper venue for exemption disputes, which must follow the VAB and circuit court track. Veterans should also confirm whether their county requires a separate annual renewal or automatic carryover of the exemption.

Florida Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Florida is governed by the Florida Statutes Title XIV — Ad Valorem Taxation; Administrative and Judicial Review (Value Adjustment Board) (Fla. Stat. Ch. 194 (§§ 194.011, 194.032, 194.171); assessment under Ch. 193; collection/tax deeds under Ch. 197). Assessment cycle: Annual. Property is assessed at just (fair market) value as of January 1. The property appraiser mails a Notice of Proposed Property Taxes (TRIM notice) in mid-to-late August showing proposed assessed value, exemptions, and estimated taxes. Assessed value: Statutory standard is 100% of just (fair market) value (Fla. Stat. § 193.011); no fractional assessment ratio. However, homestead assessed value growth is capped by Save Our Homes at the lesser of 3% or the change in CPI, and non-homestead property at 10% annually, creating an assessed-vs-market gap over time.

County Value Adjustment Board (VAB) and county Property Appraiser handle assessments/appeals; the Florida Department of Revenue, Property Tax Oversight Program, supervises and issues uniform rules and VAB procedures statewide. The window to act is short — petition to the Value Adjustment Board (VAB) must be RECEIVED (not merely postmarked) no later than 25 days after the property appraiser mails the TRIM notice. The exact deadline date is printed on each parcel's TRIM notice (typically September). Late petitions accepted only on a written showing of good cause.

A recent change to watch: Amendment 5 (approved Nov. 2024, effective Jan. 1, 2025) indexes the additional $25,000 homestead exemption to inflation ($26,411 for 2026). Separately, the 2026 Legislature placed a major property-tax constitutional amendment on the November 2026 ballot: if passed, it would exempt the first $150,000 of homestead assessed value from non-school taxes beginning Jan. 1, 2027, rising to $250,000 on Jan. 1, 2028.

How to Appeal Your Florida Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Standard $25,000 homestead exemption (all taxes) plus an additional exemption up to $25,000 on assessed value above $50,000 for non-school taxes (indexed to inflation by Amendment 5 of 2024 — $26,411 for 2026); senior (65+) additional exemption (income-limited); $5,000 exemption for veterans with 10%+ service-connected disability; total exemption for veterans with total & permanent service-connected disability; widow/widower and blind/disability exemptions. Homestead filing deadline is March 1.

First-level appeal: Informal review with the county Property Appraiser's office (optional) and/or a formal petition to the county Value Adjustment Board (VAB) under Fla. Stat. § 194.011. Petitions are heard by a special magistrate who issues a recommended decision that the VAB adopts.

Appeal deadline: Petition to the Value Adjustment Board (VAB) must be RECEIVED (not merely postmarked) no later than 25 days after the property appraiser mails the TRIM notice. The exact deadline date is printed on each parcel's TRIM notice (typically September). Late petitions accepted only on a written showing of good cause.

Next-level appeal: Judicial review by filing suit in the circuit court of the county (de novo) under Fla. Stat. § 194.171. Deadline: no action may be brought after 60 days from the date the assessment is certified for collection, or 60 days from the VAB decision.

Grounds you can raise: Just/market value exceeds actual fair market value; assessment is not uniform/equitable versus comparable properties; denial or removal of an exemption or classification (homestead, agricultural); denial of a Save Our Homes portability transfer; or a change in ownership/qualifying improvement determination.

Evidence that works: Comparable sales, independent fee appraisals, income/expense statements for income-producing property, photographs documenting condition/damage, cost or repair estimates, closing statements, and documentation of market value or exemption eligibility. The property appraiser must exchange evidence with the petitioner before the hearing on request.

How your value is assessed: Statutory standard is 100% of just (fair market) value (Fla. Stat. § 193.011); no fractional assessment ratio. However, homestead assessed value growth is capped by Save Our Homes at the lesser of 3% or the change in CPI, and non-homestead property at 10% annually, creating an assessed-vs-market gap over time.

The hearing: Quasi-judicial administrative hearing before a VAB special magistrate (or the board itself). Testimony under oath, evidence exchange, and a written recommended decision; may be in person or, in many counties, remote.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Florida

  • Just/market value overstated relative to comparable sales, especially after a purchase reset or market decline
  • Denial or retroactive removal of the homestead exemption or Save Our Homes cap (e.g., alleged non-residency)
  • Denial of Save Our Homes portability transfers or agricultural/classified-use classification

Florida Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Save Our Homes 3% assessment cap: annual increase in homestead assessed value limited to the lesser of 3% or CPI change (Fla. Const. Art. VII, § 4)
  • Portability: accumulated Save Our Homes benefit (up to $500,000) can be transferred to a new Florida homestead
  • 10% assessment cap on non-homestead/commercial property (excludes school taxes)
  • TRIM (Truth-in-Millage) notice system triggers the 25-day appeal clock rather than a tax bill

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Florida's full veteran property tax exemption?
Under Fla. Stat. § 196.081, an honorably discharged veteran who is a permanent Florida resident and has a 100% service-connected total and permanent disability certified by the VA qualifies for a complete exemption from ad valorem taxes on their homestead. Surviving spouses who have not remarried may continue the exemption, and in some cases transfer it to a new homestead. You must file Form DR-501 with the county property appraiser and provide a VA letter confirming the disability rating.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied veteran exemption in Florida?
You have 30 days from the date the property appraiser mails the notice of denial, or 30 days from the TRIM notice issued in August, to file a petition with the county Value Adjustment Board under Fla. Stat. § 194.011. Missing this deadline generally forfeits your right to challenge the denial for that tax year. If the VAB rules against you, you have 60 days to file in circuit court under Fla. Stat. § 194.171.
Can I get a refund for past years if my exemption was wrongly denied?
Yes, in many cases. Under Fla. Stat. § 197.182, Florida allows refunds of overpaid property taxes for up to four years when an exemption was improperly denied or omitted, provided you can document eligibility for those years. The county tax collector processes the refund after the property appraiser corrects the assessment roll. A demand letter requesting back-year correction can trigger this process without litigation, but supporting VA documentation for each year is essential.
Does the exemption transfer to my surviving spouse?
Yes. Under Fla. Stat. § 196.081(3), the surviving spouse of a veteran who died from service-connected causes, or who was receiving the 100% disability exemption, may continue the exemption on the homestead so long as the spouse holds title, resides on the property, and has not remarried. The exemption may also be ported to a new Florida homestead in certain circumstances. The spouse must apply with proof of marriage, the veteran's death certificate, and VA documentation.
Do I have to reapply for the veteran exemption every year?
Generally, no. Once the county property appraiser approves your veteran exemption, it typically renews automatically each year as long as you continue to own and reside on the homestead and your VA disability status remains unchanged. However, you must notify the appraiser of any change in residency, ownership, marital status (for surviving spouses), or disability rating. Some counties send annual confirmation cards; failing to return them can trigger removal of the exemption and require a new application or appeal.
What is the property tax appeal deadline in Florida?
Petition to the Value Adjustment Board (VAB) must be RECEIVED (not merely postmarked) no later than 25 days after the property appraiser mails the TRIM notice. The exact deadline date is printed on each parcel's TRIM notice (typically September). Late petitions accepted only on a written showing of good cause.
Where do I appeal my property assessment in Florida?
Informal review with the county Property Appraiser's office (optional) and/or a formal petition to the county Value Adjustment Board (VAB) under Fla. Stat. § 194.011. Petitions are heard by a special magistrate who issues a recommended decision that the VAB adopts. If unresolved, judicial review by filing suit in the circuit court of the county (de novo) under Fla. Stat. § 194.171. Deadline: no action may be brought after 60 days from the date the assessment is certified for collection, or 60 days from the VAB decision.
What property tax exemptions does Florida offer?
Standard $25,000 homestead exemption (all taxes) plus an additional exemption up to $25,000 on assessed value above $50,000 for non-school taxes (indexed to inflation by Amendment 5 of 2024 — $26,411 for 2026); senior (65+) additional exemption (income-limited); $5,000 exemption for veterans with 10%+ service-connected disability; total exemption for veterans with total & permanent service-connected disability; widow/widower and blind/disability exemptions. Homestead filing deadline is March 1.
