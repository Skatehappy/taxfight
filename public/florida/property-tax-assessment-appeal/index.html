Property Tax Assessment Appeal Letter Law in Florida

Florida property tax law is governed primarily by Chapters 192 through 197 of the Florida Statutes. Each year, county property appraisers must assess all real property at just value (fair market value) as of January 1, under Fla. Stat. § 192.042. By August, the property appraiser mails a Notice of Proposed Property Taxes, commonly called the TRIM (Truth in Millage) notice, which lists the assessed value, exemptions applied, and proposed taxes.

Under Fla. Stat. § 194.011, owners who disagree with the assessment, denial of an exemption, denial of a classification (such as agricultural), or denial of a portability transfer have the right to request an informal conference with the property appraiser and to file a petition with the county Value Adjustment Board. The deadline to file a VAB petition is 25 days after the TRIM notice is mailed for value disputes, and 30 days from the denial notice for exemption or classification denials.

The VAB is composed of county commissioners, school board members, and citizen members, and uses special magistrates to conduct evidentiary hearings. The property owner generally bears the burden of proof, but Fla. Stat. § 194.301 places the initial burden on the property appraiser to prove the assessment was made in substantial compliance with statutory requirements before the value is presumed correct.

If the VAB rules against you, Fla. Stat. § 194.171 allows you to file suit in circuit court within 60 days of the final VAB decision. You must also pay at least the amount of tax you admit in good faith to be owing before delinquency to keep your case alive. Common grounds for appeal include comparable sales showing lower market value, factual errors in property records, improper denial of Homestead or Save Our Homes cap protections, and unequal assessment compared to similar properties.

How a Demand Letter Works in Florida

A property tax appeal letter in Florida serves several strategic purposes. First, it opens an informal conference with the county property appraiser's office under Fla. Stat. § 194.011(2), where many disputes are resolved without a formal hearing. Property appraisers have authority to correct errors, restore exemptions, and adjust values when presented with credible evidence.

Your letter should clearly identify the parcel by folio or parcel ID, the tax year, the current assessed and just value, and the value or determination you believe is correct. Attach supporting evidence such as recent comparable sales within your neighborhood, a recent appraisal, photographs showing condition issues, repair estimates, income and expense statements for income-producing property, and documentation of any exemption you believe was wrongly denied.

Cite the specific statutory basis for your challenge—for example, Fla. Stat. § 193.011 for the eight criteria that must be considered in determining just value, § 196.031 for Homestead, or § 193.155 for Save Our Homes. Request a written response and an informal meeting before the VAB petition deadline.

Keep the tone factual and professional. Florida property appraisers handle thousands of inquiries during TRIM season, and a letter that is organized, evidence-based, and references the correct statutes is more likely to receive serious review. Send the letter by certified mail or through the appraiser's online portal so you have proof of delivery. Critically, sending an appeal letter does NOT extend the 25-day VAB petition deadline—file your VAB petition simultaneously to preserve your rights if the informal process does not resolve the issue.

Procedural Notes for Florida

VAB petitions are filed with the Clerk of the Value Adjustment Board in the county where the property is located. The filing fee is $15 per petition under Fla. Stat. § 194.013. Hearings are conducted by special magistrates in larger counties. If you proceed to circuit court under Fla. Stat. § 194.171, standard civil filing fees apply (typically around $400) and the 60-day deadline is jurisdictional—missing it bars the suit entirely. Florida small claims court has a jurisdictional limit of $8,000, but property tax assessment challenges generally cannot be brought there; they must go through the VAB and circuit court. You must continue paying undisputed taxes during the appeal to avoid delinquency and dismissal.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to appeal my Florida property tax assessment?
You have 25 days from the date your county mails the TRIM notice (usually in mid-to-late August) to file a petition with the Value Adjustment Board challenging the assessed value. For denials of exemptions, classifications, or portability, you have 30 days from the denial notice. These deadlines are strict. You can send an informal appeal letter to the property appraiser earlier, but doing so does not extend the VAB filing deadline, so file the petition to protect your rights.
What evidence should I include with my Florida property tax appeal letter?
Include recent comparable sales of similar properties sold near January 1 of the tax year, a professional appraisal if available, photographs documenting damage or deferred maintenance, contractor repair estimates, and any factual errors in the property record card such as wrong square footage or incorrect features. For income-producing property, attach rent rolls and operating statements. For exemption disputes, include proof of residency, ownership documents, or qualifying use evidence for agricultural classification.
Do I have to pay my property taxes while my appeal is pending?
Yes. Under Fla. Stat. § 194.014, you must pay at least 75% of the assessed taxes (for value disputes) or 100% of the non-disputed amount before they become delinquent on April 1 to keep your VAB petition or circuit court case alive. Failure to pay results in dismissal of your appeal. If you win, you receive a refund with interest under Fla. Stat. § 197.182. Always confirm the exact required payment amount with your county tax collector.
Can I appeal if I missed the Homestead Exemption deadline?
Possibly. The standard application deadline is March 1, but Fla. Stat. § 196.011(8) allows late-filed Homestead applications if you can show extenuating circumstances and file before the VAB hearing schedule closes for that year. You must petition the VAB and demonstrate you were qualified as of January 1. An appeal letter explaining your circumstances—such as illness, military service, or administrative error—paired with a timely VAB petition gives you the best chance of recovering the exemption.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal my Florida property assessment?
No. Florida law allows property owners to represent themselves before the property appraiser and the Value Adjustment Board, and many homeowners do so successfully, especially for residential value disputes. You may also be represented by a licensed real estate appraiser, attorney, CPA, or tax agent. For complex commercial properties, large-dollar disputes, or appeals headed to circuit court under Fla. Stat. § 194.171, hiring an attorney experienced in Florida property tax litigation is strongly recommended.
