Property Tax Abatement Request Law in Arizona

Arizona's property tax system is governed by Title 42 of the Arizona Revised Statutes. Each year, county assessors mail a Notice of Valuation that establishes your property's full cash value (FCV) and limited property value (LPV), along with its legal classification (Class 1 commercial, Class 3 primary residence, Class 4 rental/secondary, Class 2 agricultural, etc.). Classification matters because assessment ratios vary significantly—primary residences are assessed at 10%, while commercial property is assessed at 16% (subject to legislative adjustment).

Under A.R.S. § 42-16201, an owner who disputes the valuation or classification may file an administrative petition with the County Assessor within 60 days of the Notice of Valuation's mailing date. If the Assessor denies the petition or issues an unfavorable decision, the owner may appeal to the County Board of Equalization (or State Board of Equalization in Maricopa and Pima counties) under A.R.S. § 42-16151, and ultimately to the Arizona Tax Court under A.R.S. § 42-16203.

Separately, A.R.S. § 42-16254 provides a powerful error-correction remedy. If the property record contains a factual or clerical error—wrong square footage, incorrect classification, double assessment, property that does not exist, or a mathematical mistake—the owner can request correction for the current tax year and up to three prior tax years. This is the statutory basis for most pure abatement requests, because it allows refunds of taxes already paid.

A.R.S. § 42-11001 defines key terms like full cash value, and A.R.S. § 42-13301 et seq. govern limited property value calculations, which cap year-over-year increases at 5% for most residential property.

How a Demand Letter Works in Arizona

A Property Tax Abatement Request letter in Arizona serves two strategic purposes: it formally invokes your statutory rights and creates a documented record before any formal appeal or tax court filing. The letter should be addressed to the County Assessor's office (and copied to the County Treasurer if a refund is sought) and should clearly identify the parcel number, tax year(s) at issue, and the specific basis for relief.

Effective letters cite the controlling statute—A.R.S. § 42-16254 for error corrections, A.R.S. § 42-16201 for valuation petitions, or A.R.S. § 42-16002 for classification disputes—and attach supporting evidence. Strong evidence includes recent comparable sales, a fee appraisal, photographs documenting condition issues, building permits showing actual square footage, deed restrictions, or proof of owner-occupancy for Class 3 status.

The letter should request a specific remedy: reduction of full cash value to a stated dollar amount, reclassification to the correct legal class, abatement of the disputed tax, and a refund with statutory interest if applicable. Setting a reasonable response deadline (typically 20–30 days) signals seriousness and preserves your ability to escalate. Many Arizona assessors resolve well-supported requests informally because they prefer to avoid Board of Equalization hearings and tax court. If the Assessor refuses or fails to respond, the letter becomes Exhibit A in your administrative appeal or tax court complaint, demonstrating that you exhausted informal remedies and acted in good faith.

Procedural Notes for Arizona

Administrative appeals to the County Assessor are free. Appeals to the County or State Board of Equalization are also free. Filing in Arizona Tax Court (a division of Maricopa County Superior Court) requires a filing fee of approximately $300+ depending on case type. The Small Claims division of the Arizona Tax Court handles disputes for owner-occupied residences and properties with full cash value at or below the statutory threshold (currently $2 million for Class 3 primary residences) and offers a streamlined, less formal process. Tax court complaints must generally be filed by December 15 of the tax year, or within 60 days of a final administrative decision. Property taxes must continue to be paid while the appeal is pending to avoid delinquency penalties under A.R.S. § 42-18051.

How to Appeal Your Arizona Assessment

First-level appeal: Petition for review filed with the County Assessor within 60 days of the NOV mailing date (A.R.S. § 42-16051).

Exemptions to claim: Arizona has no general dollar homestead exemption; relief is targeted. Senior Valuation Protection ('senior freeze'): age 65+, 2+ years primary residence, 2026 income at or below $47,712 (single)/$59,640 (multiple owners) freezes the Limited Property Value for a renewable 3-year term. Exemption of $4,873 assessed value for qualifying widows/widowers, totally and permanently disabled persons, and disabled veterans (scaled by VA disability percentage). Proposition 130 (2022) restored the disabled-veteran exemption for all disabled veterans who become Arizona residents.

Appeal deadline: Administrative petition to the county assessor must be filed within 60 days after the mailing date of the Notice of Value (A.R.S. § 42-16051; USPS postmark is evidence of the filing date). The assessor must rule by August 15.

Next-level appeal: If the owner disagrees with the assessor's decision, they may petition the County Board of Equalization within 25 days of the assessor's decision (Maricopa/Pima have county BOEs; other counties use the State Board of Equalization), or bypass the board and appeal directly to Arizona Tax Court within 60 days of the assessor's decision.

Grounds you can raise: The property is valued in excess of its full cash value (over-valuation); the valuation is inequitable/not uniform compared with similar properties; error in the legal classification; or the Limited Property Value was calculated incorrectly.

Evidence that works: Owner's stated opinion of value with supporting documentation: comparable sales, recent appraisals, income/expense data for income properties, evidence of classification errors, and information showing valuation exceeds full cash value or is not uniform.

How your value is assessed: Assessment ratios are set by legal class and range from about 1% to 16%. Class 3 (owner-occupied primary residence) = 10%; Class 4 (other/rental residential) = 10%; Class 1 (commercial/industrial) = 15.5% for 2026. The ratio is applied to the Full Cash Value and Limited Property Value.

The hearing: Written petition process at the assessor level; if unresolved, a hearing before the County or State Board of Equalization; further review is a de novo proceeding in Arizona Tax Court.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Arizona

  • Full Cash Value exceeds actual market value (over-valuation), supported by comparable sales
  • Incorrect legal classification (e.g., taxed as Class 4 rental instead of Class 3 owner-occupied primary residence), which changes the tax burden
  • Denial or misapplication of exemptions/valuation freezes (senior freeze, disabled veteran, widow/widower)

Arizona Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Two separate values on the NOV: Full Cash Value (market-based) and Limited Property Value (statutorily capped); primary property taxes are levied on the LPV
  • LPV is capped so that for real property it typically increases no more than 5% over the prior year's LPV — a hedge against inflationary market-value spikes
  • Taxpayer may bypass the Board of Equalization and appeal directly to Arizona Tax Court within 60 days of the assessor's decision

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the deadline to file a property tax appeal in Arizona?
You have 60 days from the date the County Assessor mails your annual Notice of Valuation to file an administrative petition. The mailing date—not the date you received it—starts the clock. For error corrections under A.R.S. § 42-16254, you can request relief for the current tax year plus up to three prior tax years, which is much more flexible than the standard valuation appeal deadline. Tax court complaints must typically be filed by December 15 of the tax year at issue.
What is the difference between Full Cash Value and Limited Property Value?
Full Cash Value (FCV) is the Assessor's estimate of market value and is the figure you appeal when challenging an over-assessment. Limited Property Value (LPV) is a statutorily capped value used to calculate primary (school) taxes; for most residential property, LPV cannot increase by more than 5% per year regardless of market changes. Secondary taxes (bonds, overrides, special districts) are typically calculated using FCV, so reducing FCV can lower your bill even when LPV is already capped.
Can I get a refund for property taxes I already paid?
Yes, in many cases. Under A.R.S. § 42-16254, if a factual or clerical error caused you to overpay, the County can correct the roll for the current year and up to three prior tax years and issue a refund. Refunds typically include statutory interest. Pure valuation disagreements (where you simply disagree with market value) generally cannot reach back—those must be raised within the 60-day appeal window after each year's Notice of Valuation.
Do I need a lawyer to file a property tax abatement request in Arizona?
No. Property owners can handle administrative petitions to the County Assessor and Board of Equalization without an attorney, and the Small Claims division of the Arizona Tax Court is designed for self-represented owners of qualifying primary residences. However, corporations, LLCs, and trusts generally must be represented by counsel in tax court. For complex commercial valuations or classification disputes involving large dollar amounts, hiring a property tax attorney or licensed appraiser is often worthwhile.
What evidence is most persuasive in an Arizona abatement request?
The strongest evidence depends on the dispute. For valuation challenges, recent comparable sales of similar properties within the same neighborhood, an independent fee appraisal, and photographs of condition defects are most persuasive. For classification disputes, provide proof of owner-occupancy (driver's license, voter registration, utility bills) for Class 3 status, or lease agreements for rental classification. For error corrections, attach property records, surveys, building permits, or recorded deeds showing the factual mistake on the Assessor's roll.
What is the property tax appeal deadline in Arizona?
Administrative petition to the county assessor must be filed within 60 days after the mailing date of the Notice of Value (A.R.S. § 42-16051; USPS postmark is evidence of the filing date). The assessor must rule by August 15.
Where do I appeal my property assessment in Arizona?
Petition for review filed with the County Assessor within 60 days of the NOV mailing date (A.R.S. § 42-16051).
