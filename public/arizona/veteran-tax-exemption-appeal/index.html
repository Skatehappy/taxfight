Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Arizona

Arizona's veteran property tax exemption is rooted in Article IX, Section 2.2 of the Arizona Constitution, which was amended by voters in November 2022 (Proposition 130) to restore exemptions for resident veterans with service or non-service connected disabilities. The implementing statute, A.R.S. § 42-11111, provides an exemption from property tax for the primary residence or other qualifying property of a disabled veteran, subject to assessed value and income limitations that the Legislature adjusts annually for inflation. To qualify, the veteran must be an Arizona resident, have an honorable discharge, and provide documentation of disability from the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs or military branch. The exemption amount and income cap change yearly, so applicants should verify current figures with the County Assessor. Applications are filed with the County Assessor between the first Monday in January and March 1 each year under A.R.S. § 42-11152. If the assessor denies the exemption, reduces it, or fails to apply it correctly, the veteran has appeal rights under A.R.S. § 42-16051 (appeal to assessor), § 42-16101 (County Board of Equalization), and § 42-16201 (Tax Court). The assessor must provide written notice of denial, and the burden then shifts to the taxpayer to timely contest the determination. Arizona courts have held that exemption statutes are construed strictly but fairly, meaning the assessor cannot impose requirements beyond what the statute actually demands. Common denial reasons include incomplete DD-214 forms, missing VA disability rating letters, exceeding the income threshold, or owning property valued above the assessed value limit. Many of these denials are correctable through documentation rather than litigation.

How a Demand Letter Works in Arizona

A demand letter in an Arizona veteran exemption dispute serves three purposes: it creates a documented record, it triggers internal review by the assessor's legal staff, and it preserves your right to escalate to the County Board of Equalization or Tax Court. Address the letter to the County Assessor with a copy to the County Attorney's civil division. Open by identifying yourself as the property owner, the parcel number, and the tax year at issue. Cite Ariz. Const. art. IX, § 2.2 and A.R.S. § 42-11111 specifically, and attach copies of your DD-214, VA disability rating letter, Arizona driver's license or voter registration showing residency, and any prior exemption approvals. State the precise relief requested: reinstatement of the exemption, recalculation of the tax bill, and refund of any overpayment with statutory interest under A.R.S. § 42-1123. Set a clear deadline for response, typically 15 to 21 days, and warn that failure to respond will result in a formal appeal to the County Board of Equalization within the 25-day statutory window, followed by a Tax Court petition if necessary. Reference A.R.S. § 12-348, which allows recovery of attorney's fees against a state or county agency that takes a position not substantially justified. A professional, statute-specific tone signals that you understand the law and are prepared to litigate, which often prompts assessors to correct errors administratively rather than defend a weak denial in court.

Procedural Notes for Arizona

Arizona has a specialized Tax Court within the Maricopa County Superior Court that hears all state tax appeals statewide under A.R.S. § 12-161. Filing fees for Tax Court petitions run approximately $300 to $400. Small claims property tax appeals under A.R.S. § 12-172 are available for residential properties where the full cash value does not exceed certain thresholds, with a streamlined process and limited discovery. Arizona's small claims civil court limit is $3,500, but property tax small claims procedures use separate value-based criteria. The County Board of Equalization appeal must be filed within 25 days of the assessor's decision, and Tax Court petitions must generally be filed by December 15 of the tax year, or within 60 days of a Board decision.

Arizona Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Arizona is governed by the Arizona Revised Statutes, Title 42 (Taxation) — property tax valuation, classification and appeals (A.R.S. Title 42; A.R.S. § 42-16051 (assessor petition); A.R.S. § 42-15104 (notice of value)). Assessment cycle: Annual. The county assessor mails the Notice of Value (NOV) to the owner of record on or before March 1 (statutory deadline February 28) for the following tax year. Assessed value: Assessment ratios are set by legal class and range from about 1% to 16%. Class 3 (owner-occupied primary residence) = 10%; Class 4 (other/rental residential) = 10%; Class 1 (commercial/industrial) = 15.5% for 2026. The ratio is applied to the Full Cash Value and Limited Property Value.

County Assessor (valuation and first-level appeal); County Board of Equalization and Arizona State Board of Equalization (administrative appeals); Arizona Tax Court (judicial appeal); Arizona Department of Revenue (oversight); County Treasurer (billing and tax lien sales). The window to act is short — administrative petition to the county assessor must be filed within 60 days after the mailing date of the Notice of Value (A.R.S. § 42-16051; USPS postmark is evidence of the filing date). The assessor must rule by August 15.

A recent change to watch: Proposition 130 (approved November 8, 2022) consolidated Arizona's constitutional property tax exemption provisions and let the legislature set amounts/qualifications for widows/widowers, disabled persons, and disabled veterans — notably restoring the disabled-veteran exemption to all disabled veterans who become Arizona residents. The Class 1 commercial assessment ratio has continued to phase down (15.5% for 2026).

How to Appeal Your Arizona Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Arizona has no general dollar homestead exemption; relief is targeted. Senior Valuation Protection ('senior freeze'): age 65+, 2+ years primary residence, 2026 income at or below $47,712 (single)/$59,640 (multiple owners) freezes the Limited Property Value for a renewable 3-year term. Exemption of $4,873 assessed value for qualifying widows/widowers, totally and permanently disabled persons, and disabled veterans (scaled by VA disability percentage). Proposition 130 (2022) restored the disabled-veteran exemption for all disabled veterans who become Arizona residents.

First-level appeal: Petition for review filed with the County Assessor within 60 days of the NOV mailing date (A.R.S. § 42-16051).

Appeal deadline: Administrative petition to the county assessor must be filed within 60 days after the mailing date of the Notice of Value (A.R.S. § 42-16051; USPS postmark is evidence of the filing date). The assessor must rule by August 15.

Next-level appeal: If the owner disagrees with the assessor's decision, they may petition the County Board of Equalization within 25 days of the assessor's decision (Maricopa/Pima have county BOEs; other counties use the State Board of Equalization), or bypass the board and appeal directly to Arizona Tax Court within 60 days of the assessor's decision.

Grounds you can raise: The property is valued in excess of its full cash value (over-valuation); the valuation is inequitable/not uniform compared with similar properties; error in the legal classification; or the Limited Property Value was calculated incorrectly.

Evidence that works: Owner's stated opinion of value with supporting documentation: comparable sales, recent appraisals, income/expense data for income properties, evidence of classification errors, and information showing valuation exceeds full cash value or is not uniform.

How your value is assessed: Assessment ratios are set by legal class and range from about 1% to 16%. Class 3 (owner-occupied primary residence) = 10%; Class 4 (other/rental residential) = 10%; Class 1 (commercial/industrial) = 15.5% for 2026. The ratio is applied to the Full Cash Value and Limited Property Value.

The hearing: Written petition process at the assessor level; if unresolved, a hearing before the County or State Board of Equalization; further review is a de novo proceeding in Arizona Tax Court.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Arizona

  • Full Cash Value exceeds actual market value (over-valuation), supported by comparable sales
  • Incorrect legal classification (e.g., taxed as Class 4 rental instead of Class 3 owner-occupied primary residence), which changes the tax burden
  • Denial or misapplication of exemptions/valuation freezes (senior freeze, disabled veteran, widow/widower)

Arizona Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Two separate values on the NOV: Full Cash Value (market-based) and Limited Property Value (statutorily capped); primary property taxes are levied on the LPV
  • LPV is capped so that for real property it typically increases no more than 5% over the prior year's LPV — a hedge against inflationary market-value spikes
  • Taxpayer may bypass the Board of Equalization and appeal directly to Arizona Tax Court within 60 days of the assessor's decision

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Arizona's veteran property tax exemption?
Honorably discharged veterans who are Arizona residents and have a service or non-service connected disability documented by the VA or their military branch may qualify. The veteran must own and occupy the property as a primary residence in most cases, and must meet annual income and assessed property value limits set by the Legislature. These limits are adjusted yearly for inflation, so check with your County Assessor for current figures before applying or appealing a denial.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied veteran exemption in Arizona?
You generally have 25 days from the date of the assessor's notice to appeal to the County Board of Equalization under A.R.S. § 42-16101. If you miss that window, you may still file directly in Arizona Tax Court by December 15 of the tax year under A.R.S. § 42-16201. Sending a demand letter immediately upon denial preserves your options and often resolves the dispute before formal appeal deadlines expire.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal my Arizona veteran exemption denial?
No. Veterans can represent themselves before the County Assessor, the County Board of Equalization, and in the Tax Court small claims division. A demand letter you draft yourself, citing the correct statutes and attaching VA documentation, is often enough to resolve administrative errors. For complex denials involving income calculations or property valuation disputes above small claims thresholds, consulting a property tax attorney may be worthwhile, especially since A.R.S. § 12-348 allows fee recovery in some cases.
What documents should I include with my demand letter?
Include your DD-214 showing honorable discharge, a current VA disability rating letter, proof of Arizona residency such as a driver's license or voter registration, the assessor's denial notice, your most recent tax bill, and any prior years' exemption approvals. If income is at issue, attach your most recent federal tax return or a sworn affidavit of income. Organized, complete documentation often resolves the dispute faster than litigation.
Can I get a refund for past years if my exemption was wrongly denied?
Possibly. Arizona allows refunds for erroneously collected property taxes under A.R.S. § 42-16213 and related provisions, generally limited to the current year and a few prior years depending on when the error is discovered and whether you timely protested. Interest on refunds is governed by A.R.S. § 42-1123. Your demand letter should specifically request refunds for all affected tax years and cite the refund statute to put the assessor on notice.
