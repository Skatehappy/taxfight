Disability Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Georgia

Georgia provides several disability-based property tax exemptions, each with distinct eligibility rules. Under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-48, a disabled veteran with a 100% service-connected disability rating from the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs (or who is rated as entitled to receive 100% compensation due to unemployability) qualifies for an exemption from ad valorem taxes on a homestead. The exemption amount is adjusted annually and currently exceeds $100,000 in assessed value. Surviving unremarried spouses and minor children of qualifying veterans may also claim the exemption.

O.C.G.A. § 48-5-52 provides a separate homestead exemption for residents age 62 or older who are totally disabled, subject to income limits. Many counties also offer local disability homestead exemptions through local legislation that exceed the state baseline.

To qualify, applicants must file with the county tax commissioner or tax assessor by April 1 of the tax year, providing proof of disability such as a VA award letter, Social Security determination, or two physicians' affidavits where required. The Board of Tax Assessors reviews the application and issues a written decision.

If the exemption is denied or the property is improperly assessed, O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311 governs the appeal. Taxpayers have 45 days from the date of the notice to file a written appeal. Appeals can be directed to the County Board of Equalization, a hearing officer (for non-homestead property over $750,000), or to binding arbitration. Decisions of the Board of Equalization may be further appealed to Superior Court within 30 days. Georgia law requires assessors to consider all evidence submitted, and the burden shifts to the county to justify denial when the taxpayer presents substantiating disability documentation.

How a Demand Letter Works in Georgia

A demand letter in a Georgia disability exemption dispute serves three goals: it creates a documented record, triggers statutory review obligations, and often produces a quick reversal without the cost of formal proceedings. The letter should be addressed to the County Board of Tax Assessors and copied to the Tax Commissioner, citing O.C.G.A. § 48-5-48 (or the applicable subsection) and O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311.

Open by identifying the property by parcel ID, the tax year at issue, and the date of the denial or assessment notice. State the exemption claimed and attach all supporting documentation: VA disability rating letter, Social Security disability determination, physician affidavits, proof of age, deed showing homestead ownership, and prior year's tax bill if relevant. Explicitly demand that the Board reverse the denial, recalculate the assessment, and issue a corrected tax bill or refund.

Include a deadline for response, typically 14 to 21 days, and reference the 45-day appeal clock to make clear you intend to preserve your rights before the Board of Equalization if the matter is not resolved. Many Georgia county boards will reopen and correct denials administratively when presented with complete documentation in a formal letter, particularly when the original denial was based on incomplete forms rather than substantive ineligibility. If the demand is ignored, the letter becomes Exhibit A in your formal appeal and demonstrates good-faith effort to resolve the dispute.

Procedural Notes for Georgia

Georgia appeals begin with the County Board of Equalization at no cost to the taxpayer. If you appeal to Superior Court, filing fees range from approximately $200 to $230 depending on the county, and the county must pay costs if the final value is 80% or less of the Board's decision. Small claims court in Georgia (Magistrate Court) has a $15,000 jurisdictional limit and can hear refund disputes but not exemption denials, which must follow the § 48-5-311 process. The 45-day appeal deadline is jurisdictional and cannot be extended. Counties must mail assessment notices annually. Keep the certified mail receipt from your appeal letter as proof of timely filing.

Georgia Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Georgia is governed by the Georgia Ad Valorem Property Tax Law (assessment ratio) / Appeal of Assessment statute (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-7 (40% assessment ratio); O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311 (appeals)). Assessment cycle: Annual. Property is valued as of January 1 each year by the county Board of Tax Assessors; owners receive an Annual Notice of Assessment. Assessed value: 40% of fair market value (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-7), unless otherwise specified by law.

County Board of Tax Assessors (assessment) and County Board of Equalization / Hearing Officer / Arbitration (first-instance appeal); Georgia Department of Revenue (Local Government Services) provides statewide oversight and forms; Superior Court for judicial appeal. The window to act is short — 45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.

A recent change to watch: HB 92 (effective 2025+ tax years) eliminated the automatic freeze of assessed value merely upon filing an appeal — the freeze now applies only if the taxpayer wins a value reduction. HB 581 (ratified Nov 2024, effective Jan 1, 2025) created a statewide floating homestead exemption capping primary-residence taxable-value increases to inflation, with a phased local opt-out through 2029.

How to Appeal Your Georgia Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Standard homestead exemption (owner-occupied primary residence as of January 1; commonly $2,000 off assessed value, varies by county). Senior: $4,000 from state and county taxes for age 65+, with many counties adding larger local senior exemptions. Disabled veteran: exemption of the greater of $32,500 or the federal maximum ($121,812 for 2025) for 100% service-connected veterans. HB 581 statewide floating homestead exemption (effective Jan 1, 2025) caps annual taxable-value growth on primary residences to inflation, though most large metro counties opted out.

First-level appeal: Written appeal to the county Board of Tax Assessors, which reviews the appeal and notifies the taxpayer of its decision. If the Board makes no change, the appeal is automatically forwarded to the taxpayer's chosen trier of fact.

Appeal deadline: 45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.

Next-level appeal: Taxpayer elects one of: County Board of Equalization (BOE); a Hearing Officer (certified appraiser, for higher-value/commercial property); or binding Arbitration. From BOE or Hearing Officer, further appeal lies to Superior Court (arbitration decisions are final).

Grounds you can raise: Value (fair market value overstated), uniformity/equalization (assessed inconsistently with comparable properties), taxability, denial of exemption, and breach/denial of covenant (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311).

Evidence that works: Comparable sales, independent/fee appraisals, evidence of property condition or errors in the assessor's record, and documentation of uniformity with comparable assessments.

How your value is assessed: 40% of fair market value (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-7), unless otherwise specified by law.

The hearing: Administrative hearing before the Board of Equalization (three-member citizen panel), a certified appraiser Hearing Officer, or an arbitrator, depending on the taxpayer's election; de novo review available in Superior Court for BOE/Hearing Officer outcomes.

Where to File in Georgia

First, written appeal to the county Board of Tax Assessors, which reviews the appeal and notifies the taxpayer of its decision. If the Board makes no change, the appeal is automatically forwarded to the taxpayer's chosen trier of fact.

If that fails, taxpayer elects one of: County Board of Equalization (BOE); a Hearing Officer (certified appraiser, for higher-value/commercial property); or binding Arbitration. From BOE or Hearing Officer, further appeal lies to Superior Court (arbitration decisions are final).

Mind the deadline: 45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Georgia

  • Overstated fair market value / inaccurate valuation of the property
  • Lack of uniformity relative to comparable properties (equalization disputes)
  • Denial or amount of exemptions (homestead, senior, disabled-veteran) and, post-HB 92, loss of the automatic assessed-value freeze during appeals

Georgia Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Taxpayer may choose among triers of fact after the assessors: Board of Equalization, Hearing Officer, or Arbitration

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Georgia's disabled veteran property tax exemption?
Veterans with a 100% service-connected disability rating from the VA, or those rated as totally and permanently disabled due to unemployability, qualify under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-48. The exemption also extends to unremarried surviving spouses and minor children. You must own and occupy the home as your primary residence and file an application with the county tax commissioner, including a VA award letter as proof. The exemption amount is set by federal formula and adjusts annually.
How long do I have to appeal a denied disability exemption in Georgia?
You have 45 days from the date printed on the denial notice or annual assessment notice to file a written appeal under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311. This deadline is strict and cannot be extended for any reason, including illness or mail delays. File your appeal with the Board of Tax Assessors by certified mail or in person, and keep proof of the filing date. Missing this window forfeits your appeal rights for that tax year.
Can I appeal to small claims court in Georgia?
No. Georgia property tax exemption disputes must follow the statutory appeal process under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311, beginning with the County Board of Equalization, a hearing officer, or arbitration. Magistrate Court (small claims) has a $15,000 limit but lacks jurisdiction over exemption denials and assessment disputes. After exhausting the administrative process, you may appeal to Superior Court within 30 days of the Board of Equalization's decision.
What documents should I include with my appeal letter?
Include your VA disability rating letter or Social Security disability award, two physician affidavits if claiming total disability under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-52, the deed to your home, your driver's license showing the property as your address, the denial notice or assessment notice you received, and prior year's tax bill if relevant. Reference each document by exhibit number in the letter and send everything by certified mail with return receipt requested.
Will I have to pay taxes while my appeal is pending?
Yes. Under Georgia law, you must pay the temporary tax bill (typically 85% of the prior year's taxes or the current assessment, whichever applies) while the appeal is pending. If you win, the county refunds the overpayment with interest under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-380. Failing to pay the temporary bill can result in liens and interest charges. Keep payment records to support any refund claim once the exemption is granted.
What is the property tax appeal deadline in Georgia?
45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.
