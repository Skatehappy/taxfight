Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Georgia

Georgia's disabled veteran homestead exemption under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-48 provides an exemption from all ad valorem taxes for an amount that adjusts annually based on federal standards (currently over $109,986 of assessed value for 2024). The exemption applies to veterans who are 100% totally disabled, have a service-connected disability rating, are entitled to receive a statutory award from the VA for loss of vision or limbs, or who meet other specific criteria defined in the statute. The exemption extends to the unremarried surviving spouse and, in some cases, surviving minor children. There is also a separate exemption under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-52.1 for surviving spouses of service members killed in action.

To qualify, the property must be the veteran's primary legal residence (homestead). The veteran must file an application with the county tax commissioner along with proof of disability from the VA—typically a letter showing the disability rating or award status. Once granted, the exemption generally renews automatically as long as the veteran continues to occupy the homestead.

Denials commonly occur for procedural reasons: missing documentation, late filing (the deadline is April 1 in most counties), disputes over what counts as a qualifying disability, or assessor confusion about which veterans qualify. Under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311, any taxpayer aggrieved by an assessor's decision—including a denial of exemption—may appeal in writing within 45 days. The appeal can go to the County Board of Equalization, a hearing officer, or binding arbitration. If the taxpayer prevails on appeal and the assessor's valuation or denial is found to be without substantial justification, the taxpayer may recover attorney's fees and costs.

How a Demand Letter Works in Georgia

A demand letter is a powerful first step in Georgia because it forces the Board of Tax Assessors to formally reconsider before the dispute escalates. The letter should be addressed to both the County Tax Commissioner and the Board of Assessors, sent by certified mail with return receipt, and clearly labeled as a Notice of Appeal under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311.

The letter should identify the property by parcel number, state the tax year in dispute, and quote the specific subsection of O.C.G.A. § 48-5-48 that establishes eligibility. Attach copies of the VA disability award letter, DD-214, proof of homestead, and the original exemption application. If the denial was based on a misreading of the disability rating, explain plainly why the veteran qualifies under the statute's criteria—for example, total disability, individual unemployability, or loss of use of limbs.

Close the letter by demanding (1) immediate reversal of the denial, (2) retroactive application of the exemption to the affected tax years, (3) refund of any overpaid taxes with statutory interest, and (4) preservation of the right to recover attorney's fees under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311(g)(4)(B)(ii) if the matter proceeds to Superior Court. Set a reasonable response deadline—typically 14 to 21 days—before you formally pursue the Board of Equalization hearing. Many counties resolve clear-cut veteran exemption disputes administratively once a properly cited demand letter arrives, because the law is unambiguous and litigation costs the county more than simply granting the exemption.

Procedural Notes for Georgia

Appeals must be filed in writing within 45 days of the assessment notice or denial. The first level of review is the County Board of Equalization, which charges no filing fee. Alternatively, taxpayers may elect a hearing officer (for non-homestead properties over $500,000) or binding arbitration. Appeals from the Board of Equalization go to Superior Court, where filing fees typically run $200–$220 and de novo review applies. Georgia's small claims (Magistrate Court) limit is $15,000, but property tax exemption appeals do not proceed through Magistrate Court—they follow the dedicated O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311 process. Keep certified mail receipts and file-stamped copies of every document.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Georgia's disabled veteran property tax exemption?
Veterans with a 100% service-connected disability, those rated totally disabled due to individual unemployability, veterans entitled to a VA statutory award for loss of vision or limbs, and certain other categories defined in O.C.G.A. § 48-5-48 qualify. Unremarried surviving spouses and, in limited circumstances, surviving minor children may also qualify. The property must be the veteran's primary residence (homestead) in Georgia. Documentation from the VA showing the qualifying disability is required when applying.
How much is the Georgia disabled veteran exemption worth?
The exemption amount adjusts annually based on a federal index. For 2024, it exceeds $109,986 of assessed value, which translates to roughly $440,000 of fair market value at Georgia's 40% assessment ratio. For most Georgia veterans, this eliminates property taxes on their entire homestead. The exemption applies to all ad valorem taxes—county, school, municipal, and state—making it one of the most valuable veteran benefits available at the state level.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied exemption in Georgia?
You have 45 days from the date printed on the assessment notice or formal denial letter to file a written appeal under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311. Missing this deadline typically forfeits your right to appeal for that tax year, though you can reapply for the exemption in the next year. Send your appeal by certified mail and keep proof of timely filing. The 45-day clock runs from the notice date, not the date you received it.
Can I recover attorney's fees if I win my appeal?
Yes, in some cases. Under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311(g)(4)(B)(ii), if your appeal reaches Superior Court and the final determination is at least 80% in your favor compared to the assessor's position, you may recover reasonable attorney's fees and litigation costs. This fee-shifting provision creates strong leverage in demand letters because counties know that wrongful denials of clear-cut veteran exemptions can result in fee awards on top of the refund.
Do I need to reapply every year for the veteran exemption?
No. Once the disabled veteran homestead exemption is granted in Georgia, it generally renews automatically each year as long as you continue to own and occupy the property as your primary residence. However, if you move, change ownership structure, or if the surviving spouse remarries, eligibility may end and notification to the tax commissioner is required. Always confirm with your county tax commissioner that the exemption is properly recorded on your annual tax bill.
