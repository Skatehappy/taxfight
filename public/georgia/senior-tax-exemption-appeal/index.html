Senior Citizen Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Georgia

Georgia's senior citizen property tax exemption framework is layered. The base statewide exemption under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-47 provides homeowners 65 and older with a $4,000 exemption from state and county ad valorem taxes if their net income (excluding Social Security and certain retirement income up to the maximum Social Security benefit) does not exceed $10,000. Additional exemptions exist under § 48-5-47.1 for seniors 62+ meeting income limits, and § 48-5-52 provides a full exemption from school district ad valorem taxes for residents 62+ with household income under $10,000 (excluding retirement income up to the Social Security maximum).

Many Georgia counties have adopted local senior exemptions that are far more generous than the state minimums, often eliminating school taxes entirely for qualifying seniors regardless of income, or freezing assessed values. These local exemptions are authorized by constitutional amendments and codified in local legislation, so the rules vary significantly by county.

When a county Board of Tax Assessors denies an exemption application or removes a previously granted exemption, the denial must be communicated in writing. Under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311(e), the taxpayer has 45 days from the date of the notice to file a written appeal. Appeals first go to the county Board of Equalization, with further appeal rights to superior court under § 48-5-311(g). Common denial grounds include the property not being the primary residence on January 1, age documentation issues, income exceeding statutory caps, or failure to reapply when required. Georgia treats the homestead exemption as continuing year-to-year once granted, so improper removal is itself an appealable error.

How a Demand Letter Works in Georgia

A well-crafted appeal letter to the county Board of Tax Assessors can resolve many senior exemption denials before they reach the Board of Equalization. Georgia tax officials respond to letters that demonstrate specific statutory knowledge and document the qualifying facts. Your letter should identify the parcel by map and parcel number, state your age with reference to a birth certificate or driver's license, confirm the property was your legal residence on January 1 of the tax year, and either prove income compliance or cite the local exemption that has no income test.

The letter must invoke O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311(e) as the procedural basis for the appeal and identify the specific exemption statute (state or local) the assessor failed to apply. Include copies of supporting documents: prior year tax bills showing the exemption, Georgia driver's license matching the property address, federal tax returns or Social Security statements demonstrating income qualification, and the original exemption application if available.

Georgia counties operate under tight statutory timelines, and assessors often correct clear errors administratively rather than defend them at a Board of Equalization hearing. Demand a written response, request that the exemption be retroactively applied for the tax year in question, and ask for a refund of any overpayment with statutory interest. Reference O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311(g)(4)(B)(ii), which entitles taxpayers to attorney's fees if a superior court appeal reduces the assessment by more than 20%. This signals you understand the cost exposure the county faces if the matter escalates, often prompting voluntary correction.

Procedural Notes for Georgia

Appeals must be filed in writing within 45 days of the assessment or denial notice, delivered to the county Board of Tax Assessors. There is no filing fee at the Board of Equalization stage. If the Board of Equalization rules against you, you have 30 days to appeal to superior court, which requires a $25-$209 filing fee depending on the county. Taxpayers may also elect arbitration or, for non-homestead property valued over $500,000, a hearing officer. Georgia's small claims (magistrate court) limit of $15,000 generally does not apply to property tax appeals, which follow the dedicated § 48-5-311 process. Pay the temporary tax bill (typically the lesser of last year's tax or 85% of current) during appeal to avoid penalties.

Georgia Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Georgia is governed by the Georgia Ad Valorem Property Tax Law (assessment ratio) / Appeal of Assessment statute (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-7 (40% assessment ratio); O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311 (appeals)). Assessment cycle: Annual. Property is valued as of January 1 each year by the county Board of Tax Assessors; owners receive an Annual Notice of Assessment. Assessed value: 40% of fair market value (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-7), unless otherwise specified by law.

County Board of Tax Assessors (assessment) and County Board of Equalization / Hearing Officer / Arbitration (first-instance appeal); Georgia Department of Revenue (Local Government Services) provides statewide oversight and forms; Superior Court for judicial appeal. The window to act is short — 45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.

A recent change to watch: HB 92 (effective 2025+ tax years) eliminated the automatic freeze of assessed value merely upon filing an appeal — the freeze now applies only if the taxpayer wins a value reduction. HB 581 (ratified Nov 2024, effective Jan 1, 2025) created a statewide floating homestead exemption capping primary-residence taxable-value increases to inflation, with a phased local opt-out through 2029.

How to Appeal Your Georgia Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Standard homestead exemption (owner-occupied primary residence as of January 1; commonly $2,000 off assessed value, varies by county). Senior: $4,000 from state and county taxes for age 65+, with many counties adding larger local senior exemptions. Disabled veteran: exemption of the greater of $32,500 or the federal maximum ($121,812 for 2025) for 100% service-connected veterans. HB 581 statewide floating homestead exemption (effective Jan 1, 2025) caps annual taxable-value growth on primary residences to inflation, though most large metro counties opted out.

First-level appeal: Written appeal to the county Board of Tax Assessors, which reviews the appeal and notifies the taxpayer of its decision. If the Board makes no change, the appeal is automatically forwarded to the taxpayer's chosen trier of fact.

Appeal deadline: 45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.

Next-level appeal: Taxpayer elects one of: County Board of Equalization (BOE); a Hearing Officer (certified appraiser, for higher-value/commercial property); or binding Arbitration. From BOE or Hearing Officer, further appeal lies to Superior Court (arbitration decisions are final).

Grounds you can raise: Value (fair market value overstated), uniformity/equalization (assessed inconsistently with comparable properties), taxability, denial of exemption, and breach/denial of covenant (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311).

Evidence that works: Comparable sales, independent/fee appraisals, evidence of property condition or errors in the assessor's record, and documentation of uniformity with comparable assessments.

How your value is assessed: 40% of fair market value (O.C.G.A. § 48-5-7), unless otherwise specified by law.

The hearing: Administrative hearing before the Board of Equalization (three-member citizen panel), a certified appraiser Hearing Officer, or an arbitrator, depending on the taxpayer's election; de novo review available in Superior Court for BOE/Hearing Officer outcomes.

Where to File in Georgia

First, written appeal to the county Board of Tax Assessors, which reviews the appeal and notifies the taxpayer of its decision. If the Board makes no change, the appeal is automatically forwarded to the taxpayer's chosen trier of fact.

If that fails, taxpayer elects one of: County Board of Equalization (BOE); a Hearing Officer (certified appraiser, for higher-value/commercial property); or binding Arbitration. From BOE or Hearing Officer, further appeal lies to Superior Court (arbitration decisions are final).

Mind the deadline: 45 days from the date the Annual Notice of Assessment was mailed (the clock runs from the mail date, not receipt). Written appeal filed with the county Board of Tax Assessors, standard Form PT-311A.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Georgia

  • Lack of uniformity relative to comparable properties (equalization disputes)
  • Denial or amount of exemptions (homestead, senior, disabled-veteran) and, post-HB 92, loss of the automatic assessed-value freeze during appeals

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the deadline to appeal a denied senior exemption in Georgia?
You have 45 days from the date printed on the assessment notice or exemption denial letter to file a written appeal with your county Board of Tax Assessors under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-311(e). This deadline is strict—Georgia courts have repeatedly dismissed late appeals regardless of the merits. Mark the date the notice was mailed, not received, and file early. Email or hand-delivery is recommended over mail to preserve proof of timely filing.
What income counts against the Georgia senior exemption limit?
For state exemptions under O.C.G.A. § 48-5-47 and § 48-5-52, 'net income' excludes Social Security benefits and retirement income up to the maximum Social Security benefit amount (adjusted annually). Wages, interest, dividends, rental income, and pension amounts above the Social Security cap count toward the $10,000 limit. Many county-level senior exemptions use different or higher income thresholds—or none at all—so check your county's specific local exemption ordinance before assuming you don't qualify.
Can I appeal if my exemption was removed without notice?
Yes. Georgia law requires written notice of any change to your exemption status. If the county silently removed your exemption and you discover it on a tax bill, you can appeal by treating the bill itself as the notice and filing within 45 days. Cite the lack of statutory notice as an additional procedural ground for reversal. You may also request retroactive reinstatement for prior years if the removal was clearly erroneous.
Do I have to pay my property taxes while appealing?
Yes. Georgia requires payment of a 'temporary tax bill' during the appeal—generally the lesser of the prior year's tax amount or 85% of the current year's bill. Failure to pay can result in interest, penalties, and a tax lien. If your appeal succeeds, you'll receive a refund of any overpayment with statutory interest. Contact your county tax commissioner to request the temporary billing amount once your appeal is filed.
What happens if the Board of Tax Assessors denies my appeal letter?
If the Board of Tax Assessors does not grant your appeal, the matter automatically advances to the county Board of Equalization for a hearing. You'll receive notice of the hearing date, typically within 30-45 days. You can present documents and testimony. If the Board of Equalization rules against you, you have 30 days to appeal to superior court, where you can request a jury trial and recover attorney's fees if the court reduces your assessment by more than 20%.
What is the property tax appeal deadline in Georgia?
