Senior Citizen Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Ohio

Ohio's Homestead Exemption, codified at Ohio Revised Code § 323.151 through § 323.159, allows qualifying homeowners aged 65 or older, or those permanently and totally disabled, to shield $26,200 of their home's market value from property taxation. Disabled veterans receive an enhanced exemption of $52,300. To qualify, the applicant must own and occupy the home as their primary residence as of January 1 of the application year. For applicants who first applied in 2014 or later, an income means-test applies: Ohio Adjusted Gross Income (OAGI) must fall below the annually-indexed threshold (currently around $38,600 for tax year 2024). Applicants who qualified before 2014 are 'grandfathered' and not subject to the income limit.

Applications are submitted to the county auditor on Form DTE 105A. If the auditor denies the application, the applicant receives a written notice of denial. Under Ohio Revised Code § 5715.19, property owners may file a complaint with the County Board of Revision (BOR) challenging the denial or the property's valuation. The BOR is composed of the county auditor, treasurer, and a member of the Board of County Commissioners. After a BOR hearing, dissatisfied parties may appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (BTA) under Ohio Revised Code § 5717.01, or alternatively to the county Court of Common Pleas under § 5717.05. The BTA appeal must be filed within 30 days of the BOR's decision. Ohio courts have consistently held that homestead statutes should be liberally construed in favor of the taxpayer when eligibility requirements are substantially met.

How a Demand Letter Works in Ohio

A demand letter for a senior homestead exemption denial in Ohio works by formally putting the county auditor on notice that you contest their determination and intend to pursue statutory appeal rights. Because Ohio's homestead system is largely administrative, many denials stem from documentation problems—missing proof of age, unclear ownership records, or income verification issues—that can be corrected without a full BOR hearing. A clear, well-cited letter often prompts the auditor's office to reverse the denial administratively.

Your letter should: (1) identify the parcel number, property address, and tax year in dispute; (2) cite Ohio Revised Code § 323.152 establishing the exemption; (3) list specific eligibility facts—date of birth proving you are 65+, ownership documentation, and OAGI figures from your Ohio income tax return; (4) attach supporting documents like a driver's license, deed, and tax return excerpts; (5) reference the denial notice by date and stated reason; and (6) demand reconsideration within a specific timeframe, typically 14 to 21 days, before you escalate to the Board of Revision under § 5715.19.

The letter should also preserve your appeal rights by noting that you are filing—or will timely file—a formal complaint with the BOR before the March 31 deadline. Tone matters: Ohio county auditors respond better to professional, fact-driven letters than to confrontational ones. Closing with a request for written response and a clear statement of intent to appeal further if necessary creates a paper trail that strengthens any subsequent BTA or court action.

Procedural Notes for Ohio

BOR complaints must be filed by March 31 of the year following the tax year using Form DTE 1 (or DTE 105H for homestead-specific issues). There is no filing fee for BOR complaints in Ohio. Appeals to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals require a $25 filing fee and must be filed within 30 days of the BOR decision. Alternatively, appeals may go to the Court of Common Pleas. Ohio's small claims court limit of $6,000 generally does not apply to property tax exemption disputes, which follow the specialized BOR/BTA track. Hearings before the BOR are informal, but you may bring counsel, witnesses, and documentary evidence. Procedures vary slightly by county, so confirm local filing rules with your county auditor.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Ohio's senior Homestead Exemption?
Ohio homeowners aged 65 or older (or who turn 65 during the application year) who own and occupy their home as a primary residence on January 1 qualify. For applicants new since 2014, Ohio Adjusted Gross Income must fall below the indexed threshold (approximately $38,600 for 2024). Permanently disabled homeowners and disabled veterans also qualify, with veterans receiving an enhanced $52,300 exemption. Surviving spouses aged 59+ of previously qualifying homeowners may continue the exemption.
What is the deadline to appeal a homestead exemption denial in Ohio?
You must file a complaint with the County Board of Revision by March 31 of the year following the tax year in dispute under Ohio Revised Code § 5715.19. If the BOR rules against you, you have 30 days from the decision to appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals or the Court of Common Pleas. Missing these deadlines generally forfeits your appeal rights for that tax year, though you may reapply for the exemption in subsequent years.
How much money will I save with the Ohio Homestead Exemption?
The exemption reduces your home's taxable market value by $26,200, which translates to actual tax savings that vary by your local millage rate. Most qualifying Ohio seniors save between $400 and $800 per year. Disabled veterans receive a $52,300 reduction, often saving over $1,000 annually. Savings depend on your school district, county, and municipal tax rates. A successful appeal can also produce retroactive credits or refunds for the disputed tax year.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal a homestead exemption denial in Ohio?
No. Ohio's Board of Revision proceedings are designed to be accessible to non-lawyers, and many seniors successfully handle their own appeals with proper documentation. A well-drafted demand letter to the county auditor often resolves issues before a hearing is needed. However, if your case proceeds to the Board of Tax Appeals or Court of Common Pleas, or involves complex ownership structures like trusts, consulting an Ohio property tax attorney is advisable.
What documents should I include with my appeal letter?
Include a copy of the denial notice, proof of age (driver's license, birth certificate, or passport), proof of ownership (deed or property tax bill), proof of primary residence (utility bills or voter registration), and your most recent Ohio income tax return showing your OAGI. Disabled applicants should include physician certification or Social Security disability documentation. Disabled veterans should attach VA disability rating documentation. Always retain originals and send certified copies to the county auditor.
