Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Ohio

Ohio's enhanced homestead exemption for disabled veterans is codified at Ohio Revised Code §§ 323.151 through 323.157. Under R.C. 323.152(A)(2)(b), a veteran who has been discharged or released from active duty under honorable conditions and who has received a total disability rating (100%) for a service-connected disability—or is rated totally disabled based on individual unemployability—qualifies for an exemption of up to $50,000 of the true value of their homestead. Unlike the standard senior or disabled homestead exemption, the disabled veteran exemption has no income limitation. The surviving spouse of a qualifying veteran may also continue receiving the exemption under R.C. 323.152(A)(2)(c), provided the spouse occupies the home and does not remarry. Applications are filed with the county auditor on Form DTE 105I, accompanied by a letter from the VA confirming the veteran's total disability rating. The auditor must approve or deny the application and notify the applicant in writing. If denied, R.C. 5715.19 governs the formal complaint process before the County Board of Revision. The Board reviews evidence, conducts hearings, and issues decisions that can be appealed further to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals under R.C. 5717.01 or directly to the county Court of Common Pleas under R.C. 5717.05. Common reasons for denial include insufficient VA documentation, failure to demonstrate that the property is the veteran's primary residence, or auditor confusion between the disabled veteran exemption and the standard homestead exemption. Knowing which statutory subsection applies to your situation is critical when challenging a denial, because the eligibility criteria and exemption amounts differ substantially.

How a Demand Letter Works in Ohio

A demand letter is often the most efficient first step in resolving a wrongful denial of the Ohio disabled veteran homestead exemption. The letter should be addressed to the county auditor who issued the denial, with a courtesy copy to the County Board of Revision. Begin by clearly identifying yourself, the property's parcel number, and the tax year at issue. Cite the controlling statute—Ohio Revised Code § 323.152(A)(2)(b)—and attach supporting documentation: your DD-214 showing honorable discharge, the VA disability rating letter showing 100% service-connected disability or individual unemployability, and proof that the property is your primary residence (driver's license, utility bills, voter registration). State plainly why the denial was incorrect, referencing the specific eligibility criteria you meet. Request reinstatement of the exemption, a refund or credit for any overpaid taxes, and written confirmation within 30 days. A demand letter accomplishes several goals: it creates a written record of your good-faith effort to resolve the dispute, it often prompts the auditor to reconsider without forcing a Board of Revision hearing, and it frames the legal and factual issues clearly should the matter escalate. Many auditors will correct administrative errors once the statutory basis is laid out. If the auditor refuses or fails to respond, your letter becomes evidence supporting your formal complaint to the Board of Revision.

Procedural Notes for Ohio

Formal complaints challenging an exemption denial must be filed with the County Board of Revision by March 31 of the year following the tax year in dispute, per R.C. 5715.19. There is generally no filing fee for homestead exemption complaints at the Board of Revision level. Appeals from the Board of Revision to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals must be filed within 30 days of the Board's decision under R.C. 5717.01. Ohio's small claims court limit is $6,000, but property tax exemption disputes are not heard in small claims—they follow the administrative appeal track. Veterans should retain copies of all correspondence and consider requesting a hearing transcript if the matter proceeds to the Board of Tax Appeals.

Ohio Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Ohio is governed by the Ohio Revised Code Title 57 (Taxation) — real property valuation, complaints, and appeals (Chapters 5715, 5717, 5721) (Ohio Rev. Code §§ 5715.01, 5715.19, 5717.01; Ohio Admin. Code 5703-25-05). Assessment cycle: Six-year cycle: a full reappraisal every 6 years with a triennial update at the 3-year midpoint. Property is valued as of the tax lien date (January 1). Assessed value: 35% — the taxable (assessed) value of each parcel is 35 percent of true value in money (ORC § 5715.01; OAC 5703-25-05).

County Board of Revision (first level) and the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (state administrative appeal); county auditors administer valuations. The Ohio Department of Taxation oversees property tax administration statewide. The window to act is short — march 31 of the following tax year to file a valuation complaint (DTE Form 1) with the county Board of Revision — a firm statutory cutoff. To appeal a Board of Revision decision to the Board of Tax Appeals, a notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days after the BOR's decision is mailed (ORC 5717.01).

A recent change to watch: As of 2025-2026, House Bill 493 proposes ending the sale of delinquent tax liens to third parties, and the Ohio Senate has advanced a Homestead Exemption expansion. (Reform proposals — not yet enacted as of 2026-07-22.).

How to Appeal Your Ohio Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Homestead Exemption for seniors (65+) and permanently disabled homeowners: exempts the first $26,200 of home value (income-tested; OAGI $38,600 or less for first-time qualifiers in 2024). Enhanced Homestead for disabled veterans (100% service-connected): exempts the first $52,300 of value. Surviving-spouse exemption for spouses of public safety personnel killed in the line of duty.

First-level appeal: County Board of Revision (BOR) — file a DTE Form 1 complaint against valuation. The BOR is composed of the county auditor, county treasurer, and a county commissioner. An informal review with the County Auditor is often available first.

Appeal deadline: March 31 of the following tax year to file a valuation complaint (DTE Form 1) with the county Board of Revision — a firm statutory cutoff. To appeal a Board of Revision decision to the Board of Tax Appeals, a notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days after the BOR's decision is mailed (ORC 5717.01).

Next-level appeal: Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (BTA) under ORC 5717.01, appealed within 30 days of the BOR decision mailing. Further review may be taken to the Court of Appeals / Ohio Supreme Court.

Grounds you can raise: The appraised/true (market) value assigned by the county auditor is incorrect (too high or too low). You cannot challenge the 35% assessment ratio itself — only the underlying true value.

Evidence that works: Recent sale price of the subject property, comparable sales, independent appraisals, income/expense data for income-producing property, and photographs of condition/defects.

How your value is assessed: 35% — the taxable (assessed) value of each parcel is 35 percent of true value in money (ORC § 5715.01; OAC 5703-25-05).

The hearing: Administrative hearing before the county Board of Revision where the owner and auditor present evidence; the BOR issues a written decision. BTA appeals are heard de novo.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Ohio

  • Auditor's market/true value overstated relative to recent arm's-length sale price or independent appraisal
  • Failure to reflect property condition, damage, or functional/economic obsolescence
  • Income-producing property valued too high on income-approach assumptions (rents, vacancy, cap rate)

Ohio Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Four-tier appeal path: County Auditor (informal) → Board of Revision → Board of Tax Appeals → Court of Appeals/Ohio Supreme Court
  • 35% statutory assessment ratio applied uniformly — appeals may only contest true value, not the ratio
  • Six-year reappraisal cycle with a triennial (3-year) update, and a hard March 31 DTE Form 1 filing deadline

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Ohio's disabled veteran homestead exemption?
To qualify, you must be an honorably discharged veteran who has received a 100% service-connected disability rating from the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, or be rated totally disabled based on individual unemployability. You must own and occupy the home as your primary residence. Unlike the standard homestead exemption, there is no income limit. Surviving spouses of qualifying veterans may continue the exemption if they occupy the home and have not remarried. The exemption shields up to $50,000 of the home's true market value from property taxation.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied veteran exemption in Ohio?
Under Ohio Revised Code § 5715.19, you must file a formal complaint with the County Board of Revision by March 31 of the year following the tax year being disputed. For example, to challenge a denial affecting your 2024 taxes, the complaint must be filed by March 31, 2025. Sending a demand letter to the county auditor before this deadline can sometimes resolve the issue administratively. If the Board of Revision rules against you, you have 30 days to appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals.
What documents should I include with my appeal?
Include a copy of your DD-214 showing honorable discharge, your most recent VA disability rating letter confirming 100% service-connected disability or individual unemployability, proof of property ownership (deed or recent tax bill), and evidence that the home is your primary residence such as a driver's license, voter registration, or utility bills. If appealing as a surviving spouse, include the veteran's death certificate and your marriage certificate. Form DTE 105I is the official application; reference it in your appeal.
Can I get a refund for prior years if my exemption was wrongly denied?
Possibly. Ohio law allows for retroactive application of the homestead exemption in limited circumstances, particularly when the denial resulted from auditor error rather than applicant fault. R.C. 323.153 permits late applications under certain conditions. If you can demonstrate that you were eligible during prior tax years and the denial was improper, you may request a refund or credit for overpaid taxes. The county auditor or Board of Revision will determine the scope of any retroactive relief on a case-by-case basis.
Do I need an attorney to appeal a veteran exemption denial?
No, you are not required to have an attorney. Many veterans successfully handle Board of Revision complaints on their own, especially when the facts are straightforward and well-documented. However, if your case involves complex issues—such as disputed disability ratings, residency questions, or appeals to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals—consulting with an attorney experienced in Ohio property tax law or a veterans service organization may be helpful. County Veterans Service Commissions also provide free assistance with benefits-related matters, including exemption appeals.
