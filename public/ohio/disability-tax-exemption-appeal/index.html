Disability Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Ohio

Ohio's disability homestead exemption is governed by Ohio Revised Code §§ 323.151 through 323.159. To qualify, you must own and occupy your home as your primary residence on January 1 of the tax year and be permanently and totally disabled as certified by a physician, psychologist, or a state or federal agency such as the Social Security Administration or Department of Veterans Affairs. The exemption shields a portion of your home's market value (currently $26,200, indexed for inflation) from property taxation. For applications filed in 2014 or later, the standard exemption is also subject to an income means test under R.C. § 323.152, though disabled veterans rated 100% service-connected and surviving spouses of qualifying public safety officers are exempt from the income limit and receive an enhanced $52,300 reduction.

If the county auditor denies your application or revokes an existing exemption, you receive written notice with the basis for the denial. Under R.C. § 5715.19, you have the right to file a Complaint Against the Assessment with the County Board of Revision (BOR). The BOR is a three-member panel consisting of the county auditor, treasurer, and a commissioner representative. The Board holds an evidentiary hearing where you may present medical documentation, income records, and proof of ownership and occupancy.

If the BOR rules against you, R.C. § 5717.01 allows appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (BTA) within 30 days, and BTA decisions can be further appealed to the Ohio Supreme Court or the local Court of Appeals under R.C. § 5717.04. Ohio courts have consistently held that the disability certification, not the auditor's independent judgment, is controlling.

How a Demand Letter Works in Ohio

A demand letter in an Ohio disability exemption dispute serves two purposes: it creates a documented record of your good-faith effort to resolve the issue and often prompts the auditor to correct administrative errors without a formal BOR hearing. Address your letter to the County Auditor with a copy to the Board of Revision. Begin by identifying the parcel number, tax year, and the specific denial or revocation notice you received. Cite R.C. § 323.151 et seq. as the statutory basis for your exemption and attach supporting documentation: physician certification (Form DTE 105E), SSA or VA disability award letter, proof of ownership (deed), and proof of primary residence (driver's license, utility bills).

Clearly state the relief requested: reinstatement of the exemption, retroactive credit for tax years wrongly assessed (up to three prior years per R.C. § 5715.19(A)), and refund of any overpayments with statutory interest. Set a reasonable response deadline — typically 21 to 30 days — and notify the auditor that failure to respond will result in a formal Complaint Against the Assessment filed before the March 31 statutory deadline. If your dispute involves a continuing certification issue, request a written explanation of what additional documentation would resolve it. A professional, statute-grounded demand letter signals that you understand your rights and are prepared to escalate, which often results in faster administrative correction.

Procedural Notes for Ohio

BOR Complaints (Form DTE 1) must be filed by March 31 of the year following the tax year in dispute. There is no filing fee for a BOR complaint in most Ohio counties. Appeals to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals must be filed within 30 days of the BOR decision and require a $25 filing fee. Small claims court (with Ohio's $6,000 limit) is generally not the proper venue for property tax exemption disputes — the BOR and BTA have exclusive original jurisdiction. Keep certified mail receipts for all correspondence. Disabled veterans should use Form DTE 105I, and surviving spouses Form DTE 105G. County-specific procedural rules may apply, so confirm with your local auditor's office.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Ohio's disability homestead exemption?
You must own and occupy your Ohio home as your primary residence as of January 1 of the tax year and be certified as permanently and totally disabled by a physician or qualifying agency like the SSA or VA. For applications after 2013, household Ohio Adjusted Gross Income must fall below the annually adjusted threshold (approximately $38,600 for 2024). Disabled veterans rated 100% service-connected and certain surviving spouses qualify regardless of income and receive an enhanced exemption.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied disability exemption in Ohio?
You must file a Complaint Against the Assessment (Form DTE 1) with your County Board of Revision by March 31 of the year following the tax year in dispute. This deadline is jurisdictional under R.C. § 5715.19, meaning the BOR cannot hear your case if filed late. If the BOR rules against you, you have 30 days to appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals under R.C. § 5717.01.
Can I get refunds for prior years if my exemption was wrongly denied?
Yes. Under R.C. § 5715.19 and § 5713.08, if the BOR or BTA finds the auditor wrongly denied your exemption, you may receive credit or refund for up to three prior tax years, plus statutory interest on overpayments. You must specifically request retroactive relief in your complaint and provide documentation showing you would have qualified in each prior year claimed.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal to the Board of Revision?
No. Ohio law allows individual property owners to represent themselves before the Board of Revision and the Board of Tax Appeals. The process is designed to be accessible. However, if your case involves complex disability certification issues, income disputes, or substantial back-tax refunds, consulting a property tax attorney or qualified tax representative may improve your chances. Many cases are resolved at the demand letter stage without any hearing.
What documents should I attach to my demand letter?
Include your physician's disability certification (Form DTE 105E) or SSA/VA disability award letter, a copy of your deed or other proof of ownership, proof of primary residence (Ohio driver's license, utility bills, voter registration), the auditor's denial or revocation notice, and recent tax bills showing the assessment in dispute. For disabled veterans, attach your VA disability rating decision letter showing 100% permanent and total service-connected disability.
