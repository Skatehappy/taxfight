Homestead Exemption Denial Appeal Law in Ohio

Ohio's Homestead Exemption is governed by Ohio Revised Code §§ 323.151 through 323.159. The program reduces the taxable market value of an eligible homeowner's primary residence by $26,200 (adjusted annually for inflation), with an enhanced exemption of $52,300 for qualifying disabled veterans and surviving spouses of public service officers killed in the line of duty. To qualify, the homeowner generally must be at least 65 years old (or turn 65 during the application year), be permanently and totally disabled, or be a surviving spouse age 59 or older. Most applicants must also meet an Ohio adjusted gross income threshold, which is updated yearly by the Ohio Department of Taxation. Disabled veterans with a 100% service-connected disability rating are exempt from the income test.

Applications are filed with the county auditor on DTE Form 105A (or 105I for disabled veterans). If the auditor denies the application, the auditor must issue a written notice of denial, typically on DTE Form 106B, explaining the reason. Under R.C. § 323.154, the applicant may appeal the denial to the County Board of Revision. Under R.C. § 5715.19, complaints regarding valuation and certain exemption issues must generally be filed by March 31 of the following tax year, but homestead-specific denials follow the timeline stated on the auditor's notice. The Board of Revision will hold a hearing where you can present evidence such as a driver's license showing your address, federal tax returns, medical disability determinations, or VA rating decisions. Decisions of the Board of Revision can be further appealed to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals or the county court of common pleas under R.C. § 5717.01.

How a Demand Letter Works in Ohio

A demand letter is often the fastest, lowest-cost way to reverse an improper homestead denial in Ohio. Many denials result from administrative oversights — a missing signature, a misread income figure, an outdated disability certification, or an auditor's mistaken belief that the property is not owner-occupied. A clear, well-documented letter to the county auditor frequently prompts a corrected determination before any formal appeal hearing is necessary.

An effective Ohio demand letter should identify the parcel number, the tax year at issue, and the specific DTE form used for the original application. It should cite Ohio Revised Code §§ 323.151–323.153 and quote the eligibility criterion you meet (age, disability, surviving spouse, or veteran status). Attach supporting documents: proof of age, Ohio driver's license or state ID showing the property address, federal Form 1040 or Ohio IT-1040 demonstrating income eligibility, a Social Security disability award letter or physician certification (DTE Form 105E), or a VA rating decision for disabled veteran applicants.

The letter should request reconsideration within a stated timeframe (typically 14–30 days), demand restoration of the exemption retroactive to the qualifying tax year, and request a refund or credit of any overpaid taxes. State clearly that if the auditor does not reverse the denial, you intend to file a formal complaint with the County Board of Revision under R.C. § 5715.19 and, if necessary, appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals. Sending the letter by certified mail creates a record of timely written objection that protects your appeal rights.

Procedural Notes for Ohio

Appeals of homestead denials in Ohio are filed with the County Board of Revision in the county where the property is located. There is generally no filing fee for a homestead-related complaint, though valuation complaints may have nominal fees that vary by county. The Board of Revision must hear and decide the matter within 90 days of filing in most cases. Further appeals go to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (filing fee currently $25) or the court of common pleas within 30 days of the Board of Revision's decision under R.C. § 5717.01. Small claims court is generally not the proper forum for property tax exemption disputes in Ohio, even though the small claims limit is $6,000, because exemption authority rests with the auditor and Board of Revision.

Ohio Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Ohio is governed by the Ohio Revised Code Title 57 (Taxation) — real property valuation, complaints, and appeals (Chapters 5715, 5717, 5721) (Ohio Rev. Code §§ 5715.01, 5715.19, 5717.01; Ohio Admin. Code 5703-25-05). Assessment cycle: Six-year cycle: a full reappraisal every 6 years with a triennial update at the 3-year midpoint. Property is valued as of the tax lien date (January 1). Assessed value: 35% — the taxable (assessed) value of each parcel is 35 percent of true value in money (ORC § 5715.01; OAC 5703-25-05).

County Board of Revision (first level) and the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (state administrative appeal); county auditors administer valuations. The Ohio Department of Taxation oversees property tax administration statewide. The window to act is short — march 31 of the following tax year to file a valuation complaint (DTE Form 1) with the county Board of Revision — a firm statutory cutoff. To appeal a Board of Revision decision to the Board of Tax Appeals, a notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days after the BOR's decision is mailed (ORC 5717.01).

A recent change to watch: As of 2025-2026, House Bill 493 proposes ending the sale of delinquent tax liens to third parties, and the Ohio Senate has advanced a Homestead Exemption expansion. (Reform proposals — not yet enacted as of 2026-07-22.).

How to Appeal Your Ohio Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Homestead Exemption for seniors (65+) and permanently disabled homeowners: exempts the first $26,200 of home value (income-tested; OAGI $38,600 or less for first-time qualifiers in 2024). Enhanced Homestead for disabled veterans (100% service-connected): exempts the first $52,300 of value. Surviving-spouse exemption for spouses of public safety personnel killed in the line of duty.

First-level appeal: County Board of Revision (BOR) — file a DTE Form 1 complaint against valuation. The BOR is composed of the county auditor, county treasurer, and a county commissioner. An informal review with the County Auditor is often available first.

Appeal deadline: March 31 of the following tax year to file a valuation complaint (DTE Form 1) with the county Board of Revision — a firm statutory cutoff. To appeal a Board of Revision decision to the Board of Tax Appeals, a notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days after the BOR's decision is mailed (ORC 5717.01).

Next-level appeal: Ohio Board of Tax Appeals (BTA) under ORC 5717.01, appealed within 30 days of the BOR decision mailing. Further review may be taken to the Court of Appeals / Ohio Supreme Court.

Grounds you can raise: The appraised/true (market) value assigned by the county auditor is incorrect (too high or too low). You cannot challenge the 35% assessment ratio itself — only the underlying true value.

Evidence that works: Recent sale price of the subject property, comparable sales, independent appraisals, income/expense data for income-producing property, and photographs of condition/defects.

How your value is assessed: 35% — the taxable (assessed) value of each parcel is 35 percent of true value in money (ORC § 5715.01; OAC 5703-25-05).

The hearing: Administrative hearing before the county Board of Revision where the owner and auditor present evidence; the BOR issues a written decision. BTA appeals are heard de novo.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Ohio

  • Auditor's market/true value overstated relative to recent arm's-length sale price or independent appraisal
  • Failure to reflect property condition, damage, or functional/economic obsolescence
  • Income-producing property valued too high on income-approach assumptions (rents, vacancy, cap rate)

Ohio Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Four-tier appeal path: County Auditor (informal) → Board of Revision → Board of Tax Appeals → Court of Appeals/Ohio Supreme Court
  • 35% statutory assessment ratio applied uniformly — appeals may only contest true value, not the ratio
  • Six-year reappraisal cycle with a triennial (3-year) update, and a hard March 31 DTE Form 1 filing deadline

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is Ohio's homestead exemption worth?
For tax year 2024, the standard homestead exemption reduces the taxable market value of your home by $26,200. Disabled veterans with a 100% service-connected disability and qualifying surviving spouses receive an enhanced exemption of $52,300. The actual dollar savings depend on your local millage rate, but most qualifying homeowners save several hundred dollars per year. The exemption amount is adjusted annually for inflation by the Ohio Department of Taxation.
What is the income limit for Ohio's homestead exemption?
Homeowners who first applied in 2014 or later must meet an Ohio adjusted gross income limit, which is set annually by the Department of Taxation (approximately $38,600 for 2024 applications, indexed each year). Homeowners who qualified before 2014 are generally grandfathered without an income test. Disabled veterans with a 100% service-connected rating are exempt from the income limit entirely, regardless of when they applied.
How long do I have to appeal a homestead denial in Ohio?
The deadline is stated on your DTE Form 106B notice of denial — typically 60 days from the date of the notice. For broader complaints involving valuation or exemption status filed under R.C. § 5715.19, the deadline is generally March 31 of the year following the tax year in dispute. Missing the deadline usually forfeits your appeal rights for that tax year, so act quickly and send your demand letter by certified mail.
Can I get a refund for past years if my exemption was wrongly denied?
Yes, in many cases. If the County Board of Revision or a higher tribunal reverses the auditor's denial, the exemption is typically restored retroactive to the year you were eligible. You may receive a refund or tax credit for overpaid property taxes for that year. Retroactive relief for multiple prior years is more limited and depends on whether you timely applied or appealed. Document every application and denial carefully.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal a homestead exemption denial?
No. Most Ohio homeowners handle homestead appeals without an attorney. The County Board of Revision process is designed to be accessible, and you can represent yourself at the hearing. A well-drafted demand letter to the auditor often resolves the issue before any hearing. However, if the denial involves complex disability documentation, trust ownership, or you plan to appeal to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals, consulting a property tax attorney can be helpful.
