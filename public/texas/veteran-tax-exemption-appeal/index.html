Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Texas

Texas provides several distinct property tax exemptions for veterans, and each has its own rules. Under Texas Tax Code § 11.22, veterans with a service-connected disability rated between 10% and 100% receive a partial exemption ranging from $5,000 to $12,000 of the assessed value of any property they own. Under § 11.131, a veteran rated 100% disabled or determined by the VA to be unemployable due to service-connected conditions is entitled to a total exemption on their residence homestead — meaning zero property tax on that home. Section 11.132 extends a partial homestead exemption to disabled veterans with donated homes, and § 11.133 protects surviving spouses of service members killed in action or who died in the line of duty, allowing them to retain a full homestead exemption.

To qualify, the veteran must file Form 50-135 (for partial disability) or Form 50-114 with disability documentation (for the 100% homestead exemption) with the local appraisal district. The exemption applies as of January 1 of the tax year, but late applications are permitted in many cases up to five years after the delinquency date under § 11.431 and § 11.439. Surviving spouses must demonstrate they have not remarried and continue to occupy the qualifying residence.

Denials commonly occur when the appraisal district claims the disability letter is outdated, the property is not the veteran's principal residence, or the percentage rating does not match VA records. Texas Tax Code § 41.41 gives every property owner the right to protest a denial of an exemption before the Appraisal Review Board, and § 42.01 preserves the right to appeal an adverse ARB decision to district court.

How a Demand Letter Works in Texas

A demand letter in a Texas veteran exemption dispute serves two purposes: it creates a clear written record before the ARB hearing, and it often resolves the issue informally with the chief appraiser. Texas appraisal districts have authority under § 11.45 to reconsider exemption denials administratively, and many will reverse course when presented with organized documentation rather than forcing a formal hearing.

A strong letter should identify the specific exemption claimed (citing § 11.131, § 11.22, § 11.132, or § 11.133), attach the VA disability rating decision letter showing the percentage and effective date, include proof of homestead status (driver's license matching the property address), and reference the date of the denial notice. If the denial was based on a procedural defect, the letter should invoke the late-application provisions of § 11.431 or § 11.439, which allow corrections going back up to five years.

The letter should demand: (1) reinstatement of the exemption for the current tax year; (2) retroactive correction for any prior years improperly taxed; (3) a refund of overpaid taxes with statutory interest under § 31.12; and (4) written confirmation within a reasonable period, typically 14 to 21 days, before the protest deadline expires. Sending the letter by certified mail to the chief appraiser preserves proof of delivery if the matter advances to the ARB or district court.

Procedural Notes for Texas

Protests must be filed with the Appraisal Review Board by May 15 or within 30 days of the notice of denial, whichever is later (Tax Code § 41.44). There is no filing fee at the ARB level. If the ARB rules against you, you may appeal to district court within 60 days under § 42.21, or elect binding arbitration for properties valued under $5 million via § 41A. Small claims court is generally not used for exemption disputes — the ARB is the exclusive administrative forum. Surviving spouses and 100%-disabled veterans should also confirm whether the school district tax ceiling under § 11.26 applies. Attorney's fees may be recoverable in district court appeals under § 42.29 if you prevail.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if my VA disability rating changed mid-year?
Under Texas Tax Code § 11.42(e), if you become qualified for the 100% disabled veteran homestead exemption during the year, the exemption applies on a prorated basis from the date of qualification. Submit your updated VA award letter to the appraisal district immediately. If the district denies retroactive application, your demand letter should cite § 11.42(e) directly and request a corrected appraisal roll and refund of taxes paid for the qualifying portion of the year.
Can a surviving spouse of a 100% disabled veteran keep the exemption?
Yes. Under Texas Tax Code § 11.131(c), the surviving spouse of a veteran who qualified for the 100% disabled veteran exemption is entitled to continue the total homestead exemption, provided the spouse has not remarried and the property remains their residence homestead. A separate provision, § 11.133, protects spouses of service members killed in action. If the appraisal district denies continuation, the demand letter should attach the marriage certificate, the veteran's death certificate, and the prior exemption documentation.
How far back can I claim a missed veteran exemption?
Texas Tax Code § 11.431 allows late homestead exemption applications up to two years after the delinquency date, and § 11.439 specifically permits disabled veterans and surviving spouses to file late applications up to five years back. If you discover you were entitled to an exemption in prior years, your letter should request retroactive application, refund of overpaid taxes under § 31.11, and statutory interest. Documentation of VA rating effective dates is critical.
What happens if I miss the protest deadline?
Missing the May 15 or 30-day protest deadline is serious, but not always fatal. Under § 41.411, you may protest at any time before taxes become delinquent if the appraisal district failed to deliver required notice. You may also pursue a § 25.25 motion to correct the appraisal roll for clerical errors or exemption errors going back several years. A demand letter framed under these provisions can sometimes restore rights that appear lost.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal a veteran exemption denial in Texas?
No. The ARB process is designed for property owners to represent themselves, and most veteran exemption disputes are resolved at the informal conference with the appraisal district or at the ARB hearing without counsel. A well-drafted demand letter with the right statutory citations and documentation often resolves the matter before a hearing. However, if the case advances to district court under § 42.01, hiring a property tax attorney is advisable, especially because attorney's fees may be recoverable under § 42.29.
