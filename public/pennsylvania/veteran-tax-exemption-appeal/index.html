Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania's Disabled Veterans' Real Estate Tax Exemption is codified at 51 Pa. C.S. § 8901 et seq., with implementing regulations at 43 Pa. Code Chapter 5. To qualify, an applicant must: (1) have served in the U.S. Armed Forces during a period of war or armed conflict and received an honorable discharge; (2) be a resident of Pennsylvania; (3) own and occupy the property as a principal dwelling; (4) be rated 100% permanently and totally disabled by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs as a result of a service-connected disability, or be blind, paraplegic, or have lost two or more limbs; and (5) demonstrate financial need under the standards set by the State Veterans' Commission. Financial need is presumed if annual household income is at or below the threshold set annually by regulation (currently $108,046 as of recent guidance, subject to update); above that, the applicant must document need. The exemption applies to the entire real estate tax liability on the principal residence and any necessary curtilage. Surviving unmarried spouses of qualifying veterans may continue the exemption under § 8902. Applications are filed with the County Director of Veterans Affairs, who forwards them to the State Veterans' Commission for final determination. Common denial reasons include incomplete VA rating documentation, failure to prove financial need, characterization of the discharge, or disputes over whether the property is the principal residence. Each of these grounds can be challenged with proper documentation. Denials are reviewable by the State Veterans' Commission, and adverse final orders may be appealed to the Commonwealth Court under Pennsylvania's administrative agency law.

How a Demand Letter Works in Pennsylvania

A demand and appeal letter in Pennsylvania serves two purposes: it preserves your appeal rights within the 30-day window after a denial notice, and it puts the County Director of Veterans Affairs and State Veterans' Commission on notice that you intend to challenge the determination with documented evidence. An effective letter begins by identifying the specific denial reason in writing and citing 51 Pa. C.S. § 8901 along with the relevant subsection. Attach a copy of your VA benefits letter showing the 100% permanent and total disability rating, your DD-214 confirming honorable wartime service, proof of Pennsylvania residency and ownership (deed or recent tax bill), and current financial documentation if need is contested. If the denial was based on income, provide tax returns, Social Security statements, and a sworn statement of household expenses to show financial need under 43 Pa. Code § 5.24. If the denial questioned principal residence status, include utility bills, voter registration, and driver's license. Conclude by formally requesting reconsideration and, in the alternative, a hearing before the State Veterans' Commission. A clear, statute-anchored letter often resolves the matter without a contested hearing because the Commission can correct administrative errors directly. Send the letter by certified mail to both the County Director of Veterans Affairs and the State Veterans' Commission in Annville, PA, and keep proof of mailing for your file.

Procedural Notes for Pennsylvania

Appeals from a State Veterans' Commission denial must generally be filed within 30 days under 2 Pa. C.S. § 752 and Pa. R.A.P. 1512, taken to the Commonwealth Court of Pennsylvania. Filing fees at the Commonwealth Court vary; fee waivers (in forma pauperis) are available under Pa. R.C.P. 240. County assessment appeals related to the underlying property valuation follow a separate track through the County Board of Assessment Appeals with deadlines typically of August 1 or September 1 depending on the county class. Small claims (Magisterial District Court) jurisdiction in Pennsylvania is capped at $12,000 but is generally not the proper venue for exemption disputes, which proceed administratively. Always confirm current deadlines with your county.

Pennsylvania Property Tax Overview

Property tax in Pennsylvania is governed by the Consolidated County Assessment Law (Title 53, Chapter 88); appeal procedure at Section 8844 (53 Pa.C.S. § 8801 et seq. (appeals: 53 Pa.C.S. § 8844)). Assessment cycle: Base-year assessment system: counties assess at a fixed base-year value and are not required to reassess on a set statewide cycle; STEB certifies a new Common Level Ratio annually (effective July 1 through June 30) to relate base-year assessed values to current market values. Assessed value: Common Level Ratio (CLR) published annually by the State Tax Equalization Board (STEB) for each of the 67 counties. CLR is the median ratio of assessed value to market value from STEB's annual sales-ratio study. When the CLR varies by more than 15% from the county's established predetermined ratio, the board must apply the CLR to the proven market value.

County Board of Assessment Appeals / Board of Revision of Taxes (assessment appeals); State Tax Equalization Board (STEB) under the PA Department of Community & Economic Development (Common Level Ratios); county Tax Claim Bureau (tax sales); PA Department of Revenue (Property Tax/Rent Rebate). The window to act is short — set by each county; the annual appeal deadline in most counties is August 1 (some run to September 1 or October 1; Allegheny County 2027 deadline is September 1, 2026). Interim/change-of-assessment appeals: generally 40 days from the mailing date of the notice.

A recent change to watch: For 2026, STEB-published Common Level Ratios and Allegheny County-specific changes were highlighted as creating appeal opportunities (particularly for commercial property) where revised ratios lower the effective assessment; no specific statewide legislative reform to the assessment-appeal statute was verified.

How to Appeal Your Pennsylvania Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Homestead/Farmstead Exclusion reduces the taxable assessed value of an owner-occupied primary residence (lowering school district taxes); full real estate tax exemption for veterans honorably discharged and rated 100% permanently service-connected disabled; state-funded Property Tax/Rent Rebate for homeowners age 65+, widows/widowers age 50+, and disabled residents age 18+ with household income up to $45,000 (rebates up to $1,000, file PA-1000 by June 30).

First-level appeal: Written appeal to the county Board of Assessment Appeals (in Philadelphia, the Board of Revision of Taxes), which schedules a hearing and issues a decision.

Appeal deadline: Set by each county; the annual appeal deadline in most counties is August 1 (some run to September 1 or October 1; Allegheny County 2027 deadline is September 1, 2026). Interim/change-of-assessment appeals: generally 40 days from the mailing date of the notice.

Next-level appeal: Appeal to the county Court of Common Pleas (de novo); Allegheny and Philadelphia are subject to distinctive statutory provisions.

Grounds you can raise: The property's fair market value is overstated (over-assessment), and/or the assessment is non-uniform relative to comparable properties / the county's common level ratio; the appellant bears the burden of proving fair market value.

Evidence that works: Credible evidence of fair market value: recent comparable sales, the property's own recent sale price, independent/fee appraisals, income and expense data for income-producing property, and photographs/documentation of condition.

How your value is assessed: Common Level Ratio (CLR) published annually by the State Tax Equalization Board (STEB) for each of the 67 counties. CLR is the median ratio of assessed value to market value from STEB's annual sales-ratio study. When the CLR varies by more than 15% from the county's established predetermined ratio, the board must apply the CLR to the proven market value.

The hearing: Administrative hearing before the county Board of Assessment Appeals where the owner presents market-value evidence and the Board issues a decision; further review is de novo before the Court of Common Pleas.

Common Property Tax Disputes in Pennsylvania

  • Over-assessment / fair market value disputes, especially where the base-year value no longer reflects current market value
  • Non-uniformity claims and correct application of the STEB Common Level Ratio versus the county's predetermined ratio
  • Denial or loss of homestead/farmstead, senior, or disabled-veteran exemptions

Pennsylvania Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Base-year assessment system with no mandatory statewide reassessment cycle, so market shifts are corrected mainly through the STEB Common Level Ratio rather than routine reassessment
  • Appeal deadlines are set county-by-county (commonly August 1) rather than a single statewide date
  • CLR is applied to the proven market value only when it deviates more than 15% from the county's predetermined ratio — a key lever in over-assessment appeals

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for Pennsylvania's disabled veteran property tax exemption?
You must have served honorably during a period of war or armed conflict, be a Pennsylvania resident, own and live in the home as your principal residence, and have a 100% permanent and total service-connected disability rating from the VA (or be blind, paraplegic, or have lost two or more limbs). You must also demonstrate financial need, which is presumed below the income threshold set annually by the State Veterans' Commission under 43 Pa. Code § 5.24.
How long do I have to appeal a denial?
You generally have 30 days from the date of the written denial notice to file an appeal or request reconsideration with the State Veterans' Commission. If the Commission issues a final adverse order, you have another 30 days to petition for review by the Commonwealth Court of Pennsylvania under Pa. R.A.P. 1512. Missing these deadlines can permanently waive your appeal rights, so send your demand letter by certified mail immediately.
What if my income is above the financial need threshold?
You can still qualify by affirmatively proving financial need under 43 Pa. Code § 5.24. The Commission considers total household income, expenses, medical costs, dependents, and assets. Submit tax returns, bank statements, medical bills, and a detailed budget showing that paying property taxes would create hardship. Many veterans above the presumptive threshold are approved when they document recurring medical expenses or care costs related to their service-connected disability.
Does the exemption transfer to a surviving spouse?
Yes. Under 51 Pa. C.S. § 8902, the unmarried surviving spouse of a qualifying veteran may continue receiving the exemption, provided they continue to occupy the property as their principal residence and meet financial need standards. The exemption ends if the surviving spouse remarries or sells the home. Surviving spouses must file their own application and supporting documentation with the County Director of Veterans Affairs to establish continuing eligibility.
Will the exemption refund taxes I already paid?
Once approved, the exemption applies prospectively to real estate taxes on your principal residence. Pennsylvania law and county practice on retroactive refunds vary by jurisdiction; some counties refund taxes paid during the application year, while others apply the exemption only to the next billing cycle. Your appeal letter should specifically request refund of any taxes paid after your VA rating became effective and cite the effective date in your VA award letter.
What is the property tax appeal deadline in Pennsylvania?
Set by each county; the annual appeal deadline in most counties is August 1 (some run to September 1 or October 1; Allegheny County 2027 deadline is September 1, 2026). Interim/change-of-assessment appeals: generally 40 days from the mailing date of the notice.
Where do I appeal my property assessment in Pennsylvania?
Written appeal to the county Board of Assessment Appeals (in Philadelphia, the Board of Revision of Taxes), which schedules a hearing and issues a decision. If unresolved, appeal to the county Court of Common Pleas (de novo); Allegheny and Philadelphia are subject to distinctive statutory provisions.
What property tax exemptions does Pennsylvania offer?
Homestead/Farmstead Exclusion reduces the taxable assessed value of an owner-occupied primary residence (lowering school district taxes); full real estate tax exemption for veterans honorably discharged and rated 100% permanently service-connected disabled; state-funded Property Tax/Rent Rebate for homeowners age 65+, widows/widowers age 50+, and disabled residents age 18+ with household income up to $45,000 (rebates up to $1,000, file PA-1000 by June 30).
