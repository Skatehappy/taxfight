Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in New York

New York offers three primary veteran property tax exemptions. The Eligible Funds Exemption under RPTL § 458 applies to veterans who used eligible pension, bonus, or insurance funds to purchase property. The Alternative Veterans Exemption under RPTL § 458-a is the most commonly used and provides a 15% assessment reduction for wartime service, an additional 10% for combat zone service, and a further reduction equal to one-half of the service-connected disability rating, all subject to municipal maximums. The Cold War Veterans Exemption under RPTL § 458-b provides a 10% or 15% reduction for veterans who served between September 2, 1945, and December 26, 1991. To qualify, the property must be the veteran's primary residence, and the applicant must file Form RP-458-a (or the appropriate form) with the local assessor by Taxable Status Date, which is March 1 in most towns but varies in cities and villages. If an assessor denies the exemption or improperly calculates it, the veteran's first remedy is administrative review before the local Board of Assessment Review (BAR) on Grievance Day under RPTL Article 5. The grievance must be filed using Form RP-524. If the BAR denies relief, the veteran may pursue a Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) proceeding under RPTL § 730 for owner-occupied residential property, or commence a tax certiorari proceeding under RPTL Article 7 in Supreme Court. SCAR is faster, costs only $30 to file, and does not require an attorney, making it the preferred path for most homeowners challenging exemption denials.

How a Demand Letter Works in New York

A demand and grievance letter in New York serves two purposes: it formally preserves your appeal rights and often persuades the assessor to correct the error voluntarily. The letter should be addressed to the local assessor and copied to the Board of Assessment Review. Begin by identifying the property by tax map number and address, the tax year in dispute, and the specific exemption claimed (cite RPTL § 458-a or § 458-b directly). Attach proof of eligibility: a certified DD-214 showing character of discharge and dates of service, VA disability rating letter if applicable, proof of primary residency, and a copy of the original Form RP-458-a application. Clearly state the assessor's error—whether it is a complete denial, miscalculation of the percentage reduction, failure to apply the combat or disability enhancement, or improper revocation. Demand reinstatement of the exemption, recalculation of the assessment, and a refund of any overpaid taxes with interest. Reference the deadline: under RPTL § 525, grievances must be filed on or before Grievance Day. Request written confirmation within a specified timeframe, typically 14 days, and state your intention to file Form RP-524 with the BAR and pursue SCAR review under RPTL § 730 if the matter is not resolved. A clear, statute-anchored letter signals that you understand the process and are prepared to escalate, which frequently results in a quick administrative correction.

Procedural Notes for New York

Grievance Day is generally the fourth Tuesday in May for towns, but cities, villages, and counties operating under special charters have different dates—always confirm with your local assessor. SCAR petitions must be filed within 30 days after the final assessment roll is filed, with a $30 filing fee paid to the County Clerk. SCAR is limited to owner-occupied one, two, or three-family residences. Article 7 tax certiorari proceedings must be commenced within the same 30-day window and require Supreme Court filing fees. Nassau County and New York City follow different calendars and procedures, including earlier protest deadlines through the NYC Tax Commission or Nassau County Assessment Review Commission. The $10,000 small claims court limit does not apply to SCAR, which is a separate statutory proceeding.

New York Property Tax Overview

Property tax in New York is governed by the New York Real Property Tax Law (RPTL); assessment review under Article 5, judicial review under Article 7, enforcement under Article 11 (N.Y. Real Property Tax Law (RPTL) §§ 512, 524, 525 (BAR review); Article 7 (judicial review); Article 11 (enforcement)). Assessment cycle: Annual assessment roll. Assessor files a tentative assessment roll (typically May 1 in most towns), followed by Grievance Day, then a final roll (typically July 1). Property is assessed at a locally-determined uniform percentage of market value; NYC and Nassau County operate on their own class-based cycles. Assessed value: No single statewide ratio — each municipality assesses at a locally-determined uniform percentage of market value (the 'level of assessment'). ORPTS establishes an annual equalization rate / residential assessment ratio (RAR) for each municipality, used to prove unequal assessment. NYC uses a class-based system with statutory assessment percentages per tax class.

New York State Department of Taxation and Finance, Office of Real Property Tax Services (ORPTS) — oversight, equalization rates, STAR administration, and forms; assessments administered locally by municipal assessors and Boards of Assessment Review, with judicial review in the state Supreme Court. NYC assessments are administered by the NYC Department of Finance. The window to act is short — complaint (Form RP-524) must be filed with the assessor or Board of Assessment Review (BAR) on or before Grievance Day. Under RPTL § 512 the default Grievance Day is the fourth Tuesday of May (May 26, 2026), but many municipalities adopt alternate dates by local law, so confirm the local date. SCAR/Article 7 petitions must be filed within 30 days after the final roll is filed.

A recent change to watch: 2025 real property tax legislation authorizes ORPTS-driven automatic upgrade of qualifying Basic STAR recipients to Enhanced STAR without a new application, with most changes taking effect in 2026. Effective December 2025, Cold War veterans became eligible to apply for the Alternative Veterans Exemption.

How to Appeal Your New York Assessment

Exemptions to claim: STAR / School Tax Relief (Basic STAR; Enhanced STAR for seniors 65+ with limited income, RPTL § 425, now delivered largely as a credit/check for newer applicants); Senior Citizens 'Aged' exemption (RPTL § 467, income-limited); Persons with Disabilities exemption (§ 459-c); Veterans exemptions — Alternative (§ 458-a), Eligible Funds (§ 458), and Cold War (§ 458-b); agricultural, religious, and charitable exemptions. Most require a one-time application; some (e.g., Aged) require annual income recertification.

First-level appeal: Board of Assessment Review (BAR) — file Form RP-524 by Grievance Day; the BAR convenes to hear and determine complaints and mails a notice of determination (RPTL §§ 524-525).

Appeal deadline: Complaint (Form RP-524) must be filed with the assessor or Board of Assessment Review (BAR) on or before Grievance Day. Under RPTL § 512 the default Grievance Day is the fourth Tuesday of May (May 26, 2026), but many municipalities adopt alternate dates by local law, so confirm the local date. SCAR/Article 7 petitions must be filed within 30 days after the final roll is filed.

Next-level appeal: After a BAR determination, two judicial paths: (1) Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) — low-cost ($30 filing fee), no attorney required, informal, limited to owner-occupied 1-3 family residences; or (2) Tax Certiorari under RPTL Article 7 in Supreme Court — formal, for commercial/multi-unit/higher-value property. Both must be filed within 30 days of the final assessment roll.

Grounds you can raise: Four statutory grounds under RPTL § 524: (1) excessive assessment (overvaluation, or improper denial/reduction of an exemption); (2) unequal assessment (higher percentage of market value than other property); (3) unlawful assessment; and (4) misclassification.

Evidence that works: Recent comparable sales, appraisals, the property's purchase price, the municipal equalization rate / residential assessment ratio (to prove unequal assessment), income/expense data for income-producing property, and condition evidence. Article 7 proceedings typically require USPAP-compliant certified appraisals. Complaint must be on Form RP-524.

How your value is assessed: No single statewide ratio — each municipality assesses at a locally-determined uniform percentage of market value (the 'level of assessment'). ORPTS establishes an annual equalization rate / residential assessment ratio (RAR) for each municipality, used to prove unequal assessment. NYC uses a class-based system with statutory assessment percentages per tax class.

The hearing: BAR: informal in-person hearing (Grievance Day) with sworn statements. SCAR: informal hearing before a court-appointed hearing officer, no attorney needed, relaxed rules of evidence. Article 7: formal Supreme Court litigation with pleadings, appraisals, and trial.

Common Property Tax Disputes in New York

  • Overvaluation — assessed value exceeding true market value, especially after market shifts or a purchase below assessed value
  • Unequal assessment — property assessed at a higher percentage of market value than comparable properties, using the equalization rate/RAR
  • Exemption denials or reductions (STAR, senior/Aged, veterans, disability)

New York Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Statutory 'Grievance Day' (default fourth Tuesday of May, RPTL § 512) as the single annual filing window before the BAR
  • Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) — an inexpensive ($30), attorney-optional appeal track reserved for owner-occupied 1-3 family homes
  • Two-track second level: informal SCAR for eligible homes vs. formal Article 7 tax certiorari litigation for commercial/high-value property
  • Locally-set fractional assessment ratios with ORPTS equalization rates / RARs used to prove 'unequal assessment'

Frequently Asked Questions

What documents do I need to prove veteran exemption eligibility in New York?
You need a certified copy of your DD-214 showing honorable or general discharge and dates of active duty service. If you served in a combat zone, include documentation of that service such as campaign medals or expeditionary medals. Disabled veterans should attach a current VA disability rating letter. You must also prove the property is your primary residence, typically through a driver's license, voter registration, or STAR exemption records. Surviving spouses need a marriage certificate and the veteran's death certificate.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied veteran exemption in New York?
You must file a grievance with the Board of Assessment Review on or before Grievance Day, which is generally the fourth Tuesday in May for towns but varies for cities and villages. Use Form RP-524. If the BAR denies your grievance, you have 30 days from the filing of the final assessment roll to commence a Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) proceeding or an Article 7 tax certiorari case in Supreme Court.
How much can the Alternative Veterans Exemption save me?
Under RPTL § 458-a, qualified wartime veterans receive a 15% reduction in assessed value, with an additional 10% reduction for service in a combat zone, and a further reduction equal to one-half of the veteran's service-connected disability rating. Each reduction is subject to maximum dollar caps set by the local taxing jurisdiction, which vary widely. Many municipalities have adopted higher maximums for school taxes. Check with your assessor for local cap amounts applicable to your property.
Can I use Small Claims Assessment Review for an exemption denial?
Yes. SCAR under RPTL § 730 is available for owner-occupied one, two, or three-family residential properties and can be used to challenge an improperly denied or calculated veteran exemption after the BAR rules against you. The filing fee is only $30, you do not need a lawyer, and hearings are informal before a court-appointed hearing officer. SCAR is generally faster and cheaper than an Article 7 tax certiorari proceeding in Supreme Court.
What if I missed Grievance Day this year?
If you missed Grievance Day, you generally cannot challenge the current year's assessment or exemption denial through the standard process and must wait until next year's Grievance Day. However, you should still file a renewal or corrected exemption application immediately for the next assessment cycle. In limited cases involving clerical errors or unlawful entries, RPTL § 550 allows correction of the tax roll outside the grievance window. Contact your assessor promptly to discuss whether your situation qualifies.
