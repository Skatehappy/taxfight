Homestead Exemption Denial Appeal Law in New York

New York's primary-residence tax exemptions are governed by the Real Property Tax Law (RPTL). The Basic and Enhanced STAR programs under RPTL §425 reduce school property taxes for owner-occupied primary residences, with Enhanced STAR available to qualifying seniors aged 65 or older meeting income limits set annually by the Department of Taxation and Finance. The Senior Citizens Exemption (RPTL §467) provides up to a 50% reduction for low-income seniors, while RPTL §459-c offers similar relief for persons with disabilities. Veterans receive exemptions under RPTL §§458, 458-a, and 458-b.

Denials typically arise from disputes over primary residence status, income thresholds, ownership structure (such as trusts or LLCs), age or disability documentation, or late filing. Under RPTL §523, taxpayers may challenge assessment-related determinations, including exemption denials, before the local Board of Assessment Review by filing Form RP-524 (Complaint on Real Property Assessment) on or before Grievance Day, which is generally the fourth Tuesday of May, though New York City and some other localities follow different schedules.

If the BAR upholds the denial, RPTL Article 7 permits judicial review in State Supreme Court within 30 days after the final assessment roll is filed. Owner-occupants of one-, two-, or three-family residences may alternatively use Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) under RPTL §730, a simplified, low-cost process. STAR denials specifically may also be appealed administratively to the New York State Department of Taxation and Finance under RPTL §425(6). Documentation showing primary residency—driver's license, voter registration, tax returns, and utility bills—is central to most successful appeals.

How a Demand Letter Works in New York

A demand letter in a New York homestead exemption denial dispute serves several strategic purposes. First, it creates a clear written record showing you contested the denial promptly and in good faith—important if the case advances to the BAR, SCAR, or Article 7 review. Second, it gives the assessor an opportunity to reverse an obvious error, such as a misclassified residency status, an overlooked Form RP-425-IVP, or a missing senior income worksheet, without forcing a hearing.

An effective letter should identify the parcel by section-block-lot or SBL number, cite the specific exemption statute being claimed (e.g., RPTL §425 for STAR or §467 for the Senior Citizens Exemption), explain why the denial was incorrect, and attach supporting proof of primary residency, age, income, disability, or veteran status. The letter should reference the upcoming Grievance Day and request that the assessor either grant the exemption or provide a written explanation that complies with RPTL §525.

Tone matters: assessors handle thousands of parcels and respond better to organized, factual letters than to accusatory ones. Cite the controlling statute, attach RP-524 or the relevant exemption application, and request confirmation in writing. If the assessor refuses, the letter becomes Exhibit A at your BAR hearing. For STAR denials handled by the state, address the letter to the Department of Taxation and Finance and reference your STAR Credit or Exemption registration number. Always send by certified mail with return receipt to preserve proof of delivery.

Procedural Notes for New York

Grievance Day in most New York towns falls on the fourth Tuesday of May, but cities, villages, and Nassau County, Suffolk County, and New York City operate on different schedules—verify with your local assessor. Filing Form RP-524 with the BAR is free. Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) under RPTL §730 carries a $30 filing fee and is limited to owner-occupied residential property. Article 7 petitions in Supreme Court require standard civil filing fees (currently $210). The statewide small claims civil court limit is $10,000, but property tax matters generally proceed through SCAR or Article 7, not small claims court. Strict 30-day deadlines after the final roll apply to judicial review.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does New York have a homestead exemption?
Not in the traditional sense. New York instead offers several primary-residence tax relief programs, including the STAR and Enhanced STAR programs (RPTL §425), the Senior Citizens Exemption (§467), the Persons with Disabilities Exemption (§459-c), and Veterans Exemptions. These function similarly to homestead exemptions in other states by reducing property taxes on an owner-occupied primary residence. When people refer to a 'homestead exemption denial' in New York, they usually mean denial of one of these owner-occupied residence exemptions.
What is the deadline to appeal an exemption denial in New York?
You must file Form RP-524 with the local Board of Assessment Review on or before Grievance Day, which is the fourth Tuesday of May in most towns. Cities, villages, Nassau County, Suffolk County, and New York City follow different schedules. If the BAR denies your complaint, you have 30 days from the filing of the final assessment roll to seek judicial review under RPTL Article 7 or, for eligible homeowners, Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR).
What is SCAR and can I use it for an exemption denial?
Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR), authorized by RPTL §730, is a streamlined, low-cost alternative to Article 7 litigation available to owners of one-, two-, or three-family owner-occupied residences. The filing fee is $30, and you do not need an attorney. SCAR can address both excessive assessments and certain exemption issues that affect the assessed taxable value. You must first file with the BAR before filing a SCAR petition within 30 days of the final assessment roll.
Can I appeal a STAR denial separately?
Yes. STAR denials have a special administrative appeal path. Since 2016, the Basic STAR program has shifted to a state-issued credit administered by the New York State Department of Taxation and Finance, and denials of the STAR credit can be appealed directly to the Department. Enhanced STAR exemptions still administered locally can be challenged on Grievance Day through the BAR. Always check whether your STAR benefit is the credit version or the exemption version before drafting your appeal.
What evidence should I attach to my appeal letter?
Attach documentation proving you meet every element of the exemption. For primary residency, include a New York driver's license, voter registration, federal and state tax returns showing the property as your address, and utility bills. For the Senior Citizens Exemption, include proof of age and income documentation such as Form RP-467-Wkst. For disability exemptions, include award letters from SSA or another qualifying agency. For veterans exemptions, attach DD-214. Organized, labeled exhibits significantly increase your chance of reversal.
