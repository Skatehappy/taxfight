Disability Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in New York

New York's disability property tax exemption is governed primarily by Real Property Tax Law § 459-c, which authorizes municipalities, counties, and school districts to grant a partial exemption to qualifying homeowners with disabilities. To qualify, the applicant must (1) have a documented physical or mental impairment (not due to current use of alcohol or illegal drugs) that substantially limits major life activities, (2) own and primarily reside in the property, and (3) meet local income limits set by the taxing jurisdiction, which currently range up to approximately $58,400 depending on the locality's adopted sliding scale. Documentation typically includes an award letter from the Social Security Administration, Railroad Retirement Board, U.S. Postal Service, State Commission for the Blind, or a physician's certification. The exemption percentage scales with income: lower incomes receive up to a 50% reduction in assessed value, while higher qualifying incomes receive smaller percentages down to 5%. Initial applications must be filed with the local assessor by Taxable Status Date, which is March 1 in most towns (different in cities and Nassau County). If the assessor denies the application, reduces the exemption, or fails to renew it, the property owner may file a written grievance with the Board of Assessment Review under RPTL Article 5, Title 1-A. Grievance Day falls on the fourth Tuesday in May for most towns, but cities and villages set their own dates. If the BAR denies relief, the homeowner may pursue Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) under RPTL § 730 for one-, two-, or three-family owner-occupied homes, or a tax certiorari proceeding under RPTL Article 7 in Supreme Court. SCAR petitions must be filed within 30 days after the final assessment roll is filed.

How a Demand Letter Works in New York

A demand letter in a New York disability exemption dispute serves three strategic purposes: it creates a clear written record, frames the legal and factual basis for relief, and signals that you are prepared to escalate. The letter should be addressed to the local assessor with a copy to the Board of Assessment Review chair. Open by identifying the property by tax map number and address, the tax year at issue, and the specific exemption denied or reduced. Cite RPTL § 459-c directly and quote the eligibility criteria you satisfy. Attach proof of disability (SSA award letter, physician certification, or equivalent), proof of ownership and primary residence, and income documentation showing you fall within the locality's adopted income brackets. Identify the assessor's specific error, whether it is a misreading of income, failure to apply the correct sliding-scale percentage, improper denial of renewal, or refusal to accept qualifying disability documentation. Request a specific remedy: restoration of the exemption, correction of the assessment roll, and refund or credit for any overpaid taxes. Set a reasonable response deadline tied to Grievance Day so you preserve your right to file Form RP-524 with the BAR. Close by stating that if the issue is not resolved, you will pursue all remedies including BAR review, SCAR, and Article 7 proceedings. A professional, statute-grounded letter often prompts the assessor to correct the roll voluntarily, especially when the disability documentation is unambiguous.

Procedural Notes for New York

Form RP-524 (Complaint on Real Property Assessment) must be filed with the Board of Assessment Review on or before Grievance Day. There is no fee to file with the BAR. If denied, Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) under RPTL § 730 is available for owner-occupied one- to three-family homes; the filing fee is $30 and the petition must be filed within 30 days after the final assessment roll is published. SCAR hearings are informal and do not require an attorney. For larger or commercial properties, a tax certiorari petition under Article 7 must be filed in Supreme Court within the same 30-day window. Nassau County, New York City, and certain cities follow different timelines and assessment calendars.

New York Property Tax Overview

Property tax in New York is governed by the New York Real Property Tax Law (RPTL); assessment review under Article 5, judicial review under Article 7, enforcement under Article 11 (N.Y. Real Property Tax Law (RPTL) §§ 512, 524, 525 (BAR review); Article 7 (judicial review); Article 11 (enforcement)). Assessment cycle: Annual assessment roll. Assessor files a tentative assessment roll (typically May 1 in most towns), followed by Grievance Day, then a final roll (typically July 1). Property is assessed at a locally-determined uniform percentage of market value; NYC and Nassau County operate on their own class-based cycles. Assessed value: No single statewide ratio — each municipality assesses at a locally-determined uniform percentage of market value (the 'level of assessment'). ORPTS establishes an annual equalization rate / residential assessment ratio (RAR) for each municipality, used to prove unequal assessment. NYC uses a class-based system with statutory assessment percentages per tax class.

New York State Department of Taxation and Finance, Office of Real Property Tax Services (ORPTS) — oversight, equalization rates, STAR administration, and forms; assessments administered locally by municipal assessors and Boards of Assessment Review, with judicial review in the state Supreme Court. NYC assessments are administered by the NYC Department of Finance. The window to act is short — complaint (Form RP-524) must be filed with the assessor or Board of Assessment Review (BAR) on or before Grievance Day. Under RPTL § 512 the default Grievance Day is the fourth Tuesday of May (May 26, 2026), but many municipalities adopt alternate dates by local law, so confirm the local date. SCAR/Article 7 petitions must be filed within 30 days after the final roll is filed.

A recent change to watch: 2025 real property tax legislation authorizes ORPTS-driven automatic upgrade of qualifying Basic STAR recipients to Enhanced STAR without a new application, with most changes taking effect in 2026. Effective December 2025, Cold War veterans became eligible to apply for the Alternative Veterans Exemption.

How to Appeal Your New York Assessment

Exemptions to claim: STAR / School Tax Relief (Basic STAR; Enhanced STAR for seniors 65+ with limited income, RPTL § 425, now delivered largely as a credit/check for newer applicants); Senior Citizens 'Aged' exemption (RPTL § 467, income-limited); Persons with Disabilities exemption (§ 459-c); Veterans exemptions — Alternative (§ 458-a), Eligible Funds (§ 458), and Cold War (§ 458-b); agricultural, religious, and charitable exemptions. Most require a one-time application; some (e.g., Aged) require annual income recertification.

First-level appeal: Board of Assessment Review (BAR) — file Form RP-524 by Grievance Day; the BAR convenes to hear and determine complaints and mails a notice of determination (RPTL §§ 524-525).

Appeal deadline: Complaint (Form RP-524) must be filed with the assessor or Board of Assessment Review (BAR) on or before Grievance Day. Under RPTL § 512 the default Grievance Day is the fourth Tuesday of May (May 26, 2026), but many municipalities adopt alternate dates by local law, so confirm the local date. SCAR/Article 7 petitions must be filed within 30 days after the final roll is filed.

Next-level appeal: After a BAR determination, two judicial paths: (1) Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) — low-cost ($30 filing fee), no attorney required, informal, limited to owner-occupied 1-3 family residences; or (2) Tax Certiorari under RPTL Article 7 in Supreme Court — formal, for commercial/multi-unit/higher-value property. Both must be filed within 30 days of the final assessment roll.

Grounds you can raise: Four statutory grounds under RPTL § 524: (1) excessive assessment (overvaluation, or improper denial/reduction of an exemption); (2) unequal assessment (higher percentage of market value than other property); (3) unlawful assessment; and (4) misclassification.

Evidence that works: Recent comparable sales, appraisals, the property's purchase price, the municipal equalization rate / residential assessment ratio (to prove unequal assessment), income/expense data for income-producing property, and condition evidence. Article 7 proceedings typically require USPAP-compliant certified appraisals. Complaint must be on Form RP-524.

How your value is assessed: No single statewide ratio — each municipality assesses at a locally-determined uniform percentage of market value (the 'level of assessment'). ORPTS establishes an annual equalization rate / residential assessment ratio (RAR) for each municipality, used to prove unequal assessment. NYC uses a class-based system with statutory assessment percentages per tax class.

The hearing: BAR: informal in-person hearing (Grievance Day) with sworn statements. SCAR: informal hearing before a court-appointed hearing officer, no attorney needed, relaxed rules of evidence. Article 7: formal Supreme Court litigation with pleadings, appraisals, and trial.

Common Property Tax Disputes in New York

  • Overvaluation — assessed value exceeding true market value, especially after market shifts or a purchase below assessed value
  • Unequal assessment — property assessed at a higher percentage of market value than comparable properties, using the equalization rate/RAR
  • Exemption denials or reductions (STAR, senior/Aged, veterans, disability)

New York Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Statutory 'Grievance Day' (default fourth Tuesday of May, RPTL § 512) as the single annual filing window before the BAR
  • Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) — an inexpensive ($30), attorney-optional appeal track reserved for owner-occupied 1-3 family homes
  • Two-track second level: informal SCAR for eligible homes vs. formal Article 7 tax certiorari litigation for commercial/high-value property
  • Locally-set fractional assessment ratios with ORPTS equalization rates / RARs used to prove 'unequal assessment'

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for the New York disability property tax exemption?
You qualify under RPTL § 459-c if you own and primarily reside in the property, have a documented physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity, and meet your locality's income limit. Acceptable proof of disability includes an SSA disability award letter, Railroad Retirement disability certification, U.S. Postal Service disability certification, a Certificate from the State Commission for the Blind, or a qualifying physician's statement. The disability cannot be based on current alcohol or illegal drug use.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied disability exemption?
You must file Form RP-524 with the Board of Assessment Review on or before Grievance Day, which is the fourth Tuesday in May in most New York towns. Cities, villages, and Nassau County follow different schedules, so check your local calendar. If the BAR denies your grievance, you have 30 days from the filing of the final assessment roll to file a SCAR petition or an Article 7 tax certiorari proceeding. Missing these deadlines typically forfeits your right to challenge that year's assessment.
How much can the disability exemption reduce my taxes?
Under RPTL § 459-c, the exemption reduces your assessed value by up to 50% if your income is at or below the lowest bracket adopted by your locality. Localities may adopt a sliding scale, with smaller reductions (down to 5%) for incomes up to roughly $58,400, depending on which option your county, city, town, village, or school district has adopted. The actual tax savings depend on your local tax rate and the assessed value of your home.
Can I use Small Claims Assessment Review for a disability exemption denial?
Yes, if your property is an owner-occupied one-, two-, or three-family residential structure used exclusively for residential purposes. SCAR under RPTL § 730 is designed to be accessible without an attorney, costs $30 to file, and is decided by a court-appointed hearing officer. You must first file a grievance with the BAR before pursuing SCAR. The petition must be filed within 30 days after the final assessment roll is filed in your municipality.
Do I need a lawyer to send the appeal letter or file a grievance?
No. New York's grievance and SCAR processes are specifically designed for self-represented homeowners. You can draft and send an appeal letter, file Form RP-524, and represent yourself before the Board of Assessment Review and a SCAR hearing officer. However, for complex cases, large refund claims, or Article 7 tax certiorari proceedings in Supreme Court, consulting an attorney experienced in New York property tax assessment disputes is often worthwhile.
