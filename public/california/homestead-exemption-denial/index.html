Homestead Exemption Denial Appeal Law in California

California's homeowners' exemption is governed primarily by Revenue and Taxation Code section 218, which reduces the assessed value of an owner-occupied principal residence by $7,000. To qualify, the claimant must own and occupy the property as their principal place of residence as of 12:01 a.m. on January 1 of the tax year. Eligibility extends to properties held in revocable living trusts and certain life estates under R&TC § 218(b). Claims are filed using BOE Form 266 with the county assessor.

When an assessor denies an exemption, R&TC § 253.5 requires written notice explaining the reason. The denial may be based on late filing (which can still entitle the claimant to 80% of the exemption under R&TC § 275), failure to occupy, multiple exemption claims, or ownership structure issues. Trust-held properties sometimes trigger erroneous denials when the assessor misreads the trust instrument.

Two separate remedies exist. First, an informal appeal directly to the assessor under R&TC § 5096 allows correction of erroneous assessments and refund of overpaid taxes for up to four years. Second, a formal application for changed assessment may be filed with the county Assessment Appeals Board under R&TC § 1603, generally within 60 days of the notice of assessed value or supplemental notice (some counties allow filing between July 2 and September 15 or November 30, depending on whether the county has adopted the extended period).

If the exemption is restored after taxes were paid, the county must issue a refund with interest under R&TC § 5151. Property owners may also pursue a refund action in superior court under R&TC § 5140 after exhausting administrative remedies.

How a Demand Letter Works in California

A demand letter to the county assessor is often the fastest, lowest-cost path to reversing a homestead exemption denial in California. Because most denials stem from administrative misunderstandings rather than substantive ineligibility, a clear written demand with documentation can resolve the issue before any formal Assessment Appeals Board hearing.

An effective letter should: (1) identify the parcel by Assessor's Parcel Number (APN) and property address; (2) cite Revenue and Taxation Code § 218 and confirm owner-occupancy as of January 1 of the relevant tax year; (3) attach proof of residency such as a California driver's license, voter registration, utility bills, or vehicle registration showing the property address; (4) if the property is held in trust, attach the relevant trust pages showing the claimant as a present beneficiary or trustor-occupant under R&TC § 218(b); (5) reference R&TC § 5096 to demand correction of the erroneous assessment and refund under § 5151; and (6) state a clear deadline for response, typically 30 days, before escalating to the Assessment Appeals Board under R&TC § 1603 or filing a refund action under § 5140.

Send the letter by certified mail with return receipt to create a documented record. Copy the county Auditor-Controller and Tax Collector to ensure all relevant offices are on notice. Even when the assessor refuses to reverse, the demand letter preserves arguments and shows the Appeals Board you acted in good faith and attempted informal resolution.

Procedural Notes for California

California Assessment Appeals Board filing fees vary by county, typically ranging from $30 to $50 per application; some counties (such as Los Angeles) charge no fee. The formal appeal deadline is generally 60 days from the date of a supplemental or escape assessment notice, or during the regular filing period (July 2 through September 15 or November 30, depending on the county). For pure exemption disputes not tied to value, R&TC § 5096 refund claims may be filed within four years. Small claims court (California limit $12,500) generally cannot be used against a county for property tax disputes; refund actions must proceed under R&TC § 5140 in superior court after exhausting administrative remedies. Always verify current deadlines with your specific county assessor's office.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is the California homeowners' exemption worth?
Under Revenue and Taxation Code § 218, qualifying owner-occupants receive a $7,000 reduction in the assessed value of their principal residence. At California's roughly 1% base property tax rate, that translates to about $70 in annual tax savings, though the exact amount depends on local voter-approved bonds and assessments. While the dollar savings are modest, losing the exemption can also signal larger assessment problems worth challenging, and the exemption renews automatically each year once granted.
What is the deadline to appeal a homestead exemption denial in California?
If the denial is part of a regular or supplemental assessment notice, you generally have 60 days from the notice date to file a formal application for changed assessment with the county Assessment Appeals Board under R&TC § 1603. For refund claims based on erroneous denial, R&TC § 5096 allows up to four years. Deadlines vary slightly by county, so confirm with your local assessor or clerk of the board immediately to avoid forfeiting your right to appeal.
Can I get the exemption if my home is in a living trust?
Yes. R&TC § 218(b) expressly allows the homeowners' exemption for properties held in revocable trusts where the trustor or a present beneficiary occupies the home as their principal residence. Denials based on trust ownership are common but often reversible. Your appeal letter should attach the relevant trust pages showing the occupant's beneficial interest and right of occupancy. Irrevocable trusts and certain LLC or corporate ownerships may not qualify, but most family living trusts do.
What if I filed my exemption claim late?
Under R&TC § 275, if you file BOE Form 266 after the February 15 deadline but on or before December 10, you are entitled to 80% of the exemption (a $5,600 reduction) for that year. Full exemption applies the following year. If your assessor denied even the partial late-filed exemption, your appeal letter should specifically cite § 275 and demand the partial reduction plus a refund of any overpaid taxes with interest under R&TC § 5151.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal a homestead exemption denial?
No. California's Assessment Appeals Board process is designed to be accessible to property owners without attorneys. Most homestead exemption denials are resolved through a written demand letter to the assessor with supporting documentation. If the assessor refuses, you can file the appeal application yourself for a small fee. An attorney may be useful for complex trust, ownership, or refund-action cases under R&TC § 5140, but routine exemption restoration usually does not require legal representation.
