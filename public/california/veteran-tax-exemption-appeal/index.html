Veteran Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in California

California provides two distinct veteran property tax exemptions. The basic Veterans' Exemption under Revenue and Taxation Code § 205 exempts $4,000 of assessed value for qualifying veterans whose total property does not exceed $5,000 (single) or $10,000 (married). Because the asset cap is low, most veterans use the much more valuable Disabled Veterans' Exemption under § 205.5. That exemption applies to the principal place of residence of a veteran who is blind in both eyes, has lost the use of two or more limbs, or is rated 100% disabled (or compensated at the 100% rate due to unemployability) by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs. Surviving spouses who have not remarried may also qualify. For 2024 the basic exemption amount is approximately $175,298, increasing to roughly $262,950 if household income falls below the annually adjusted low-income threshold; both amounts are indexed each year by the Board of Equalization under § 205.5(g). Claims are filed with the county assessor on BOE-261-G (initial) or BOE-261-GNT (annual low-income certification). If the assessor denies the claim, reduces the amount, or assesses the property without applying the exemption, the veteran may file an Application for Changed Assessment with the county Assessment Appeals Board under R&T Code §§ 1603–1605. Refund claims for taxes already paid are governed by §§ 5096–5097 and generally must be filed within four years of payment. Article XIII, § 4 of the California Constitution authorizes these exemptions and requires liberal construction in favor of qualifying veterans.

How a Demand Letter Works in California

A California veteran exemption demand letter has two jobs: trigger administrative reconsideration by the assessor and preserve every statutory right before deadlines run. Start by identifying the parcel, the tax year, and the specific exemption claimed under § 205 or § 205.5. Attach proof of eligibility — the VA disability rating letter showing 100% or unemployability status, DD-214, marriage or death certificates for surviving spouses, and the filed BOE-261-G form. Quote the controlling statute and Article XIII, § 4 of the California Constitution, and remind the assessor that exemption statutes must be liberally construed in favor of the veteran. Identify the specific error: late filing that should be granted at 80% or 90% under § 276, miscalculation of the low-income threshold, failure to apply the exemption to a surviving spouse, or denial based on documentation the VA has already issued. Demand a written response within 30 days, correction of the assessment roll, and a refund with interest under § 5151. Close by stating that if the assessor does not correct the record, you will file a timely Application for Changed Assessment with the Assessment Appeals Board and a refund claim under §§ 5096–5097. Send the letter by certified mail, return receipt requested, and copy the Clerk of the Board of Supervisors. A clear, statute-anchored letter often resolves the issue at the assessor level, avoiding a formal hearing while still locking in your appeal deadline.

Procedural Notes for California

Assessment Appeals Board applications are filed with the Clerk of the Board of Supervisors in the county where the property is located. The standard filing window is July 2 through September 15 or November 30, depending on whether your county sends value notices to all property owners (R&T Code § 1603). Filing fees vary by county, typically $30–$50 per application. The Board generally must hear the appeal within two years (§ 1604). If you lose, you may pay the tax under protest and file a refund action in California Superior Court within six months under § 5141; veteran exemption disputes often exceed the $12,500 small claims limit, so most refund suits proceed as limited or unlimited civil cases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for California's Disabled Veterans' Exemption?
Under Revenue and Taxation Code § 205.5, you qualify if the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs has rated you 100% service-connected disabled, compensates you at the 100% rate because of individual unemployability, or you are blind in both eyes or have lost the use of two or more limbs due to service. Unremarried surviving spouses of qualifying veterans, and spouses of service members who died in the line of duty, may also claim the exemption on their principal residence.
What is the deadline to appeal a denied veteran exemption in California?
You generally have 60 days from the date the assessor mails the notice of denial or supplemental assessment to file an Application for Changed Assessment with the county Assessment Appeals Board. For regular roll disputes, the filing window is July 2 through September 15 or November 30, depending on the county. Refund claims for taxes already paid must be filed within four years under §§ 5096–5097. Missing these deadlines usually forfeits your right to challenge the assessment.
Can I still get the exemption if I filed late?
Yes, partially. Revenue and Taxation Code § 276 allows late-filed Disabled Veterans' Exemption claims, but the exemption is reduced. A claim filed after the regular deadline but before the next January 1 is generally granted at 90%, and claims filed later receive 85%. Retroactive claims back to the effective date of the VA rating are allowed if filed within specific statutory periods. A demand letter citing § 276 can recover prior-year taxes you did not know were exempt.
Do I have to pay the disputed tax while my appeal is pending?
Yes. California follows a 'pay first, litigate later' rule. To preserve your right to a refund and to avoid penalties and interest, you must timely pay the property tax even while your Assessment Appeals Board case or refund claim is pending. If you win, the county must refund the overpayment with interest at the rate set by Revenue and Taxation Code § 5151. Failing to pay can also result in delinquency penalties that the appeal will not erase.
Should I hire a lawyer or use small claims court?
Most California veteran exemption disputes start administratively before the county Assessment Appeals Board, where you do not need a lawyer and there are no formal rules of evidence. If the Board rules against you, you can pay under protest and sue for a refund in Superior Court within six months under § 5141. Because multi-year disputes typically exceed the $12,500 small claims limit, refund suits generally proceed as limited or unlimited civil cases, and an attorney experienced in property tax appeals is helpful.
