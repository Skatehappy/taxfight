Disability Property Tax Exemption Appeal Law in California

California's property tax exemptions for people with disabilities are governed primarily by Revenue and Taxation Code § 205.5, which creates the Disabled Veterans' Property Tax Exemption. To qualify, the veteran must be rated 100% service-connected disabled by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, or be compensated at the 100% rate due to unemployability, and the property must be the claimant's principal place of residence. Unmarried surviving spouses may also qualify under specified conditions. The exemption amount is adjusted annually for inflation by the State Board of Equalization, and a higher 'low-income' tier applies when household income falls below the statutory threshold (also adjusted annually).

Separately, the Homeowners' Exemption under Rev. & Tax. Code § 218 provides a $7,000 reduction in assessed value for any owner-occupied home, and Propositions 19, 60, and 110 allow qualifying disabled homeowners to transfer their property tax base to a replacement primary residence anywhere in California.

When an assessor denies an exemption, the claimant has two overlapping remedies. First, under Rev. & Tax. Code § 270, late-filed exemption claims can still receive 80%–90% of the exemption value, and retroactive relief is available for up to four prior years under § 5097 if the claimant can prove eligibility. Second, the homeowner can file an Application for Changed Assessment with the county Assessment Appeals Board under Rev. & Tax. Code § 1603, challenging the assessor's valuation or refusal to apply the exemption. The Appeals Board acts as the local board of equalization and has authority to order refunds, correct the roll, and apply the exemption retroactively when supported by VA documentation, medical records, or income verification.

How a Demand Letter Works in California

A demand letter to a California county assessor works because most exemption denials stem from clerical errors, missed deadlines, or misreading of VA disability ratings—issues that can be cured administratively without a formal hearing. Your letter should open by identifying the parcel number (APN), the tax year(s) at issue, and the specific exemption claimed. Cite Rev. & Tax. Code § 205.5 for disabled veterans or the relevant disability-related provision, and attach supporting documentation: the VA rating decision letter showing 100% disability or unemployability, proof of principal residence (driver's license, voter registration, utility bills), and, if applicable, household income documentation for the low-income tier.

Next, explain precisely what the assessor got wrong—failure to recognize a 100% unemployability rating as qualifying, denial based on a late filing despite § 270's partial exemption rule, or refusal to apply the exemption retroactively under § 5097. Demand a specific remedy: full exemption for the tax year, refund of overpaid taxes with interest under § 5151, and correction of the assessment roll.

Set a reasonable response deadline (typically 30 days) and state clearly that if the assessor does not correct the roll, you will file a formal Application for Changed Assessment with the Assessment Appeals Board within the statutory window, and may pursue a refund action in superior court under Rev. & Tax. Code § 5140. A professional, statute-cited letter signals that you understand your rights and are prepared to escalate, which often prompts the assessor's exemptions unit to re-review the file and grant relief without a contested hearing.

Procedural Notes for California

Assessment appeals in California are filed with your county's Assessment Appeals Board (or Board of Supervisors sitting as a board of equalization). The standard filing window runs July 2 through September 15 in counties where the assessor mails notices to all taxpayers by August 1, and July 2 through November 30 in all other counties; supplemental and escape assessments must be appealed within 60 days of the notice date. Filing fees vary by county, typically $30–$50, and some counties waive fees for disability or low-income claimants. The Appeals Board must hear the case within two years of filing under Rev. & Tax. Code § 1604. Small claims court (limit $12,500) generally cannot adjudicate property tax exemption disputes—refund actions must be filed in superior court after exhausting administrative remedies.

California Property Tax Overview

Property tax in California is governed by the California Revenue and Taxation Code (property taxation), implementing Article XIII A of the CA Constitution (Proposition 13) (Cal. Const. art. XIII A (Prop 13); Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code §§ 110, 619, 1603, 3691 et seq.). Assessment cycle: Annual lien date of January 1 each year; under Prop 13 the base-year (purchase-price) value is set at acquisition and capped at a maximum 2% inflationary increase per year unless there is a change in ownership or new construction that triggers reassessment. Assessed value: 100% of full cash value (fair market value). Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 401 requires assessment at full value. Under Prop 13 the taxable value is the factored base-year value (acquisition value + 2%/yr max), not annual market value.

County Assessor (assessment) and county Assessment Appeals Board / county Board of Equalization (appeals) at the local level; the California State Board of Equalization (BOE) provides statewide oversight, guidance, and property tax rules. The window to act is short — regular assessment appeals filing period runs July 2 through either September 15 or November 30 depending on the county. As of 2025, 11 counties (incl. Alameda, San Francisco, Santa Clara, Ventura) use September 15; the other 47 counties use November 30. Supplemental and escape assessments must be appealed within 60 days of the notice/tax bill mailing date. No extensions.

A recent change to watch: Proposition 19 (passed Nov. 2020; base-year-value transfers effective April 1, 2021) lets homeowners 55+, severely and permanently disabled, or victims of wildfire/disaster transfer their Prop 13 base-year value to a replacement home anywhere in California up to three times, while narrowing the parent-child reassessment exclusion largely to a primary residence the transferee occupies.

How to Appeal Your California Assessment

Exemptions to claim: Homeowners' Exemption: $7,000 reduction in assessed value of an owner-occupied principal residence. Veterans' Exemption: up to $4,000. Disabled Veterans' Exemption: roughly $161,083 basic / $241,627 low-income for 2026 (inflation-adjusted). Property Tax Postponement (age 62+, blind, or disabled) and Prop 19 base-year-value transfers for homeowners 55+, severely disabled, or disaster victims. Prop 13 caps assessed-value growth at 2%/yr.

First-level appeal: Application for Changed Assessment filed with the clerk of the county Assessment Appeals Board (AAB), or in smaller counties the county Board of Equalization sitting as the appeals board (Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 1603).

Appeal deadline: Regular assessment appeals filing period runs July 2 through either September 15 or November 30 depending on the county. As of 2025, 11 counties (incl. Alameda, San Francisco, Santa Clara, Ventura) use September 15; the other 47 counties use November 30. Supplemental and escape assessments must be appealed within 60 days of the notice/tax bill mailing date. No extensions.

Next-level appeal: After exhausting the administrative appeal before the Assessment Appeals Board, the taxpayer's further remedy is a refund action in California Superior Court (judicial review), typically after paying the tax and filing a refund claim; there is no intermediate second administrative appeal body.

Grounds you can raise: Assessor's full cash value (fair market value) exceeds actual market value; incorrect base-year value; decline-in-value (Prop 8) below the factored Prop 13 base value; improper change-in-ownership or new-construction reassessment; supplemental or escape assessment disputes.

Evidence that works: Comparable sales, appraisals, income/expense data (income approach), cost data, and evidence of fair market value as of the valuation date; the § 110(b) rebuttable presumption is that full cash value equals actual purchase price in an open-market transaction.

How your value is assessed: 100% of full cash value (fair market value). Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 401 requires assessment at full value. Under Prop 13 the taxable value is the factored base-year value (acquisition value + 2%/yr max), not annual market value.

The hearing: In-person (and increasingly virtual) evidentiary hearing before the county Assessment Appeals Board; taxpayer and assessor present evidence and testimony, and the board renders a value determination.

Common Property Tax Disputes in California

  • Decline-in-value (Prop 8) claims that current market value is below the factored Prop 13 base-year value
  • Change-in-ownership and new-construction reassessments that reset the base-year value
  • Supplemental and escape assessments (60-day appeal window) after transfers or construction

California Property Tax Provisions Worth Knowing

  • Proposition 13 acquisition-value system: property is taxed on its base-year (purchase) value with a maximum 2% annual increase, not on current market value, so appeals often turn on base-year value or Prop 8 decline-in-value
  • Two county-deadline system (September 15 for 11 designated counties vs. November 30 for the other 47)
  • Proposition 8 temporary decline-in-value reassessments allow reduction when market value falls below the factored Prop 13 base value
  • Tax-deed (not tax-lien-certificate) state with a five-year defaulted period before power-to-sell

Frequently Asked Questions

Who qualifies for California's Disabled Veterans' Property Tax Exemption?
You qualify if the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs rates you 100% service-connected disabled, or pays you at the 100% rate due to individual unemployability, and the property is your principal place of residence. Unmarried surviving spouses of qualifying veterans, and surviving spouses of service members who died in the line of duty, may also qualify. A higher exemption tier applies if your household income falls below the annually adjusted low-income threshold published by the State Board of Equalization.
What if I missed the filing deadline for my exemption?
California Revenue and Taxation Code § 270 allows late-filed disabled veterans' exemption claims to receive 80% to 90% of the full exemption value, depending on how late the filing is. You can also seek retroactive relief for up to four prior tax years under § 5097 if you can prove you were eligible during those years. A demand letter citing these provisions often persuades the assessor to apply partial or retroactive relief without requiring a formal Appeals Board hearing.
How long do I have to appeal a denial in California?
For regular annual assessments, you must file an Application for Changed Assessment between July 2 and either September 15 or November 30, depending on whether your county mails value notices to all taxpayers. For supplemental assessments, escape assessments, and roll corrections, the deadline is 60 days from the date of the notice. Missing these deadlines generally bars formal appeal, though refund claims under § 5097 may still be available for up to four years.
Can I recover interest on overpaid property taxes?
Yes. Under Revenue and Taxation Code § 5151, interest accrues on property tax refunds at the county pool apportioned rate, calculated from the date of payment until the date the refund is issued. If the Appeals Board or assessor grants your exemption retroactively, the county must refund the overpaid taxes plus statutory interest. Your demand letter should specifically request both the principal refund and § 5151 interest to preserve that right.
Do I need a lawyer to appeal a property tax exemption denial?
No. California's Assessment Appeals Board process is designed to be accessible to homeowners without counsel, and many disability exemption disputes are resolved at the assessor level through a clear, well-documented demand letter. However, if your case involves complex income calculations, contested VA ratings, or large multi-year refund claims, consulting a property tax attorney or qualified tax agent registered with the State Board of Equalization can be valuable, especially before a formal Appeals Board hearing.
